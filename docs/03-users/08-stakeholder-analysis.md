## Document Scope

This document and Document 07 — Registry Participants examine some of the same entities from different analytical perspectives. That reappearance is intentional. It is not redundancy.

**Document 07** answers one question: *How does this participant interact with the Institutional Record?* It defines the behavioral and operational relationships that individuals and professionals have with the property's Institutional Record — what they contribute, what they consume, and what stewardship responsibilities they carry.

**Document 08** answers a different question: *What institutional relationship does this stakeholder have with Haus Registry?* It defines the enduring relationships between Haus Registry as a platform and the organizations, institutions, and industry bodies whose interests, authority, or activities intersect with the Registry's mission.

Some entities appear in both documents because they simultaneously participate in the Registry and hold broader institutional relationships with the platform. A contractor, for example, is a participant in the Institutional Record (Doc 07) and a Record Ecosystem Partner with institutional obligations and dependencies (Doc 08). Those are complementary perspectives, not duplicate content.

This convention — that reappearance across documents signals a change in analytical perspective rather than a repetition of content — is a repository standard established here and carried forward throughout the documentation system.

---

## Analytical Framework

Every stakeholder in this document is examined through eight questions. The framework is defined once here and applied consistently throughout.

1. **What is their relationship to Haus Registry?** The nature of the institutional connection.
2. **Why does that relationship exist?** The underlying condition that makes the relationship necessary or inevitable.
3. **What do they contribute?** Records, authority, legitimacy, data, distribution, or standards that strengthen the Registry.
4. **What do they receive?** Value, efficiency, risk reduction, or market advantage the Registry provides.
5. **What responsibilities do they carry?** Obligations — formal or informal — that the relationship creates.
6. **What dependencies exist?** Where each party relies on the other to function effectively.
7. **Where are incentives aligned or in conflict?** Honest assessment of where interests converge and where they diverge.
8. **What risks emerge if the relationship fails?** Consequences for the Registry, the stakeholder, and the Institutional Record.

## Part I — Foundational Institutions

Foundational Institutions are organizations whose authority, records, or standards underpin the Registry's legitimacy. The Registry does not create authority — it assembles, references, and reflects the authority that these institutions already hold. Their records are the primary sources that give the Institutional Record its evidentiary foundation.

This category is listed first because it reflects a constitutional principle: Haus Registry derives legitimacy from existing institutions before it creates value for the market. The Registry is designed to complement existing infrastructure, not replace it.

---

### Municipal Governments and Permit Authorities

**Strategic Importance:** Foundational

Municipal governments are the authoritative source for permit records — one of the highest-value record types in the Institutional Record. A permitted improvement is a documented, inspected, and legally authorized change to a property. No other participant can create that record. Only the municipality can.

**What is their relationship to Haus Registry?**
Municipal governments are primary source institutions. Their permit records, inspection sign-offs, and code compliance documentation are S1-level sources in the Trust Framework — the highest source tier. The Registry references, indexes, and surfaces these records; it does not create or replace them.

**Why does that relationship exist?**
Permit records are among the most reliable and consequential records a property can carry. They are created by disinterested third parties, they document physical changes to the property, and they carry legal standing. A Registry without access to municipal permit data is materially incomplete.

**What do they contribute?**
Permit issuance and inspection records, code compliance documentation, zoning and land use records, certificate of occupancy filings, and violation histories. These are irreplaceable primary sources that no private party can replicate or substitute.

**What do they receive?**
Municipalities receive no direct commercial value from the Registry in its early form. Over time, the relationship may evolve: a Registry that surfaces complete permit histories creates indirect value for municipalities by reducing duplicate record requests, supporting property tax assessment accuracy, and improving disclosure compliance at the point of sale.

**What responsibilities do they carry?**
Municipalities carry the responsibility of maintaining accurate, accessible, and complete public records. Their records are only as useful to the Registry as they are complete and current. Municipalities that maintain poor records or restrict public access create gaps in the Institutional Record that no downstream participant can repair.

**What dependencies exist?**
The Registry depends on municipal records for its highest-confidence source tier. Municipalities depend on no specific Registry function — the relationship is currently asymmetric. That asymmetry is a structural vulnerability the Registry must account for.

**Where are incentives aligned or in conflict?**
Aligned: both parties benefit when property records are complete, accurate, and accessible. Municipalities have a legal and civic obligation to maintain these records; the Registry has a commercial and mission obligation to surface them. In conflict: municipalities vary widely in their digitization, accessibility, and openness to third-party indexing. Some jurisdictions actively restrict access to public records in ways that directly limit the Registry's source quality.

**What risks emerge if the relationship fails?**
If municipal records become inaccessible — through legal restriction, system failure, or policy change — the Registry loses its primary source tier for permit and compliance documentation. The Registry Confidence Score for affected properties falls, and the Registry Verified badge becomes harder to sustain. This is an existential risk to the Registry's evidentiary foundation in affected markets.

### Regulators

**Strategic Importance:** Foundational

Regulators — including state real estate commissions, consumer financial protection bodies, data privacy authorities, and disclosure enforcement agencies — define the legal environment in which the Registry operates. They do not participate in the Institutional Record, but they determine what the Registry is permitted to do, how it must handle data, and what obligations attach to verified records used in transactions.

**What is their relationship to Haus Registry?**
Regulators are boundary-setting institutions. They do not partner with the Registry, but they govern the conditions under which the Registry operates. Their rules shape product design, data handling, disclosure language, and liability exposure.

**Why does that relationship exist?**
The Registry operates at the intersection of real estate transactions, consumer data, financial decisions, and property disclosure — all of which are heavily regulated domains. Regulatory compliance is not optional; it is a condition of operation.

**What do they contribute?**
Regulatory frameworks provide clarity — sometimes constraining, sometimes enabling. Disclosure requirements, for example, create a documented obligation that the Registry's verified records can satisfy. Data privacy regulations define what the Registry may store, share, and retain, and for how long.

**What do they receive?**
Regulators receive no direct value from the Registry. Over time, a Registry that improves disclosure accuracy and reduces fraudulent or incomplete property representations may reduce regulatory burden in markets where it operates at scale.

**What responsibilities do they carry?**
Regulators carry the responsibility of creating frameworks that are clear, consistent, and proportionate. Regulatory ambiguity — particularly around verified records used in disclosure — creates compliance risk for the Registry and uncertainty for its stakeholders.

**What dependencies exist?**
The Registry depends on regulatory clarity to design compliant products. Regulators depend on nothing from the Registry directly, though they may eventually develop expectations about how platforms like the Registry handle property data in transaction contexts.

**Where are incentives aligned or in conflict?**
Aligned: both parties benefit from accurate, complete property disclosure. Regulators want disclosure compliance; the Registry creates the infrastructure that makes compliance easier and more verifiable. In conflict: regulatory fragmentation across jurisdictions creates compliance complexity that increases operational cost and limits the Registry's ability to operate uniformly across markets.

**What risks emerge if the relationship fails?**
Regulatory non-compliance — whether through negligence or ambiguity — exposes the Registry to enforcement action, reputational damage, and forced product changes. In the most severe scenario, regulatory action in a major market could require the Registry to withdraw verified record functionality, materially damaging the platform's value proposition.

### Standards Organizations

**Strategic Importance:** Important

Standards organizations — including property data standards bodies, inspection certification organizations, and real estate data interoperability groups — define the vocabulary, formats, and protocols that govern how property information is created, shared, and interpreted across the industry.

**What is their relationship to Haus Registry?**
Standards organizations are framework institutions. Their standards define how property data is structured, what inspection reports must contain, and how records are formatted for interoperability. The Registry operates within those standards and, over time, may participate in shaping them.

**Why does that relationship exist?**
The Registry aggregates records from multiple sources — inspectors, contractors, municipalities, manufacturers — each of whom operates within industry-defined standards. Without alignment to those standards, the Registry cannot reliably classify, compare, or verify records across sources.

**What do they contribute?**
Data schemas, inspection report formats, property classification systems, and interoperability protocols. Their frameworks reduce the Registry's data normalization burden and increase the credibility of records that conform to recognized standards.

**What do they receive?**
Standards organizations benefit when platforms like the Registry implement their standards at scale. Adoption increases the relevance and durability of their frameworks. Over time, the Registry may become a reference implementation for property record standards in the residential market.

**What responsibilities do they carry?**
Standards organizations carry the responsibility of maintaining frameworks that are practical, current, and widely adoptable. Standards that are outdated, overly complex, or poorly documented create integration friction and reduce the quality of records that flow into the Institutional Record.

**What dependencies exist?**
The Registry depends on recognized standards to validate record formats and support interoperability with upstream data sources. Standards organizations depend on adoption — without platforms that implement their frameworks, standards become theoretical rather than operational.

**Where are incentives aligned or in conflict?**
Broadly aligned. Both parties benefit from well-structured, interoperable property data. Conflict is most likely to arise when standards bodies are controlled by incumbent industry participants whose interests diverge from open, verifiable property records.

**What risks emerge if the relationship fails?**
If the Registry departs from recognized standards — or if standards evolve in ways the Registry does not follow — data interoperability degrades and the cost of maintaining the Institutional Record increases. In markets where standards compliance is a regulatory expectation, non-conformance may create additional legal exposure.

## Part II — Record Ecosystem Partners

Record Ecosystem Partners are organizations that create, maintain, or enrich the Institutional Record. They are the operational contributors to the Registry's core value — the entities whose work produces the records that make properties knowable. Without them, the Institutional Record is thin. With them, it accumulates.

---

### Contractors

*The contractor's operational relationship with the Institutional Record is defined in Doc 07 — Registry Participants. This section examines the contractor's institutional relationship with Haus Registry as a Record Ecosystem Partner.*

**Strategic Importance:** Critical

**What is their relationship to Haus Registry?**
Contractors are the Registry's most important record-generating partners. Their work produces the most specific, granular, and irreplaceable records in the Institutional Record — scope of work, materials, warranties, permits, and completion dates. No other participant has direct knowledge of what was physically done to a property and how. The Registry's ability to build a complete Institutional Record is directly dependent on contractor participation.

**Why does that relationship exist?**
Contractors perform the physical work that changes properties. Every material change — a roof replacement, an HVAC installation, a foundation repair — is a record-generating event. If that event is not documented at the time it occurs, the information is degraded or lost. The Registry exists at that moment of documentation.

**What do they contribute?**
Work completion records, material specifications, warranty documentation, permit numbers, subcontractor records, and photographic evidence of completed work. These are the primary source records for the most consequential changes a property undergoes.

**What do they receive?**
Professional documentation of their work that follows the property permanently. A verifiable record of completed work — accessible through Registry Pro — becomes a professional credential that survives the client relationship. Over time, contractors who document consistently through the Registry build a verifiable body of work that distinguishes them from undocumented competitors.

**What responsibilities do they carry?**
Contractors carry the responsibility of accurate, complete, and timely documentation. A contractor who submits inaccurate records — wrong materials, incorrect dates, omitted permits — introduces error into the Institutional Record that may persist for decades and affect future owners, buyers, and inspectors. Documentation accuracy is a professional obligation, not merely an administrative task.

**What dependencies exist?**
The Registry depends on contractor participation to generate the record types that cannot be produced by any other participant. Contractors depend on the Registry to make their documentation accessible, permanent, and professionally credible rather than paper-based and perishable.

**Where are incentives aligned or in conflict?**
Aligned: contractors who document their work build verifiable professional reputations. The Registry provides that documentation infrastructure. In conflict: documentation takes time, and many contractors operate in markets where documentation is not yet a competitive differentiator. Early adoption requires behavioral change without immediate commercial reward. This tension resolves as Registry adoption increases and documented contractors gain measurable market advantage.

**What risks emerge if the relationship fails?**
If contractors do not participate, the Registry's most granular record type — physical work documentation — is absent or incomplete. The Institutional Record becomes a document archive rather than a living record of the property's condition. The Registry Confidence Score for properties without contractor documentation reflects this gap, and the Registry's value proposition at transaction time is weakened.

### Home Inspectors

*The inspector's operational relationship with the Institutional Record is defined in Doc 07 — Registry Participants. This section examines the inspector's institutional relationship with Haus Registry as a Record Ecosystem Partner.*

**Strategic Importance:** Critical

**What is their relationship to Haus Registry?**
Inspectors are the Registry's primary source of dated, professional condition assessments. An inspection report attached to the Institutional Record is among the highest-value records the Registry can contain — it is third-party verified, professionally credentialed, condition-specific, and timestamped. No other participant produces this record type.

**Why does that relationship exist?**
Inspection reports are the closest approximation the residential market has to a professional audit of a property's condition at a point in time. Their value to the Institutional Record is not limited to the transaction moment — a longitudinal series of inspection reports across multiple ownership periods creates a documented condition history that dramatically increases the Registry's evidentiary depth.

**What do they contribute?**
Dated professional condition assessments, system-level findings, documented deficiencies, and repair recommendations. Inspection reports submitted to the Registry become permanent reference points — baselines against which future conditions can be compared and future claims can be evaluated.

**What do they receive?**
Access to the property's documented history before arriving on site — system ages, prior inspection findings, permit records, and contractor documentation through Registry Pro. This access improves assessment accuracy, reduces redundant investigation, and positions the inspector's report as an addition to a longitudinal record rather than an isolated document.

**What responsibilities do they carry?**
Inspectors carry the professional responsibility of accurate, complete, and unbiased reporting. A report submitted to the Institutional Record becomes a permanent record reviewed by future stewards, buyers, agents, and insurers. The professional standards governing inspection practice apply with heightened consequence when reports become part of the permanent record.

**What dependencies exist?**
The Registry depends on inspector participation to generate condition documentation that carries professional authority. Inspectors depend on the Registry to make their reports permanently accessible and professionally attributed rather than transaction documents filed and forgotten.

**Where are incentives aligned or in conflict?**
Aligned: inspectors who submit reports to the Registry build a permanent, attributed professional record. The Registry amplifies the professional value of their work beyond the transaction. In conflict: some inspectors may resist permanent record submission if they are concerned about long-term liability exposure for findings that are later disputed. This concern must be addressed in the Registry's terms of participation and liability framework.

**What risks emerge if the relationship fails?**
Without inspector participation, the Institutional Record lacks professional condition documentation. The Registry becomes dependent on homeowner-reported and contractor-reported records — both of which carry lower verification levels than third-party professional assessments. The Registry Confidence Score reflects this limitation, and the Registry's credibility in transaction contexts is reduced.

### Manufacturers and Warranty Providers

**Strategic Importance:** Important

Manufacturers of appliances, HVAC systems, roofing materials, windows, and building components are the authoritative source for product specifications, installation requirements, warranty terms, and recall notices. Their records document what was installed in a property and what obligations and protections attach to those installations.

**What is their relationship to Haus Registry?**
Manufacturers are product record authorities. Their product documentation — specifications, warranty terms, recall notices, expected lifespans — completes the Institutional Record at the component level. The Registry provides manufacturers with a permanent, property-anchored distribution channel for product documentation that survives the original sale and the original owner.

**Why does that relationship exist?**
Manufacturers produce products that live in properties for years or decades. Their warranty and recall obligations follow the product, not the original purchaser — but they have no reliable mechanism for reaching subsequent owners. The Registry, anchored to the property rather than the person, solves this distribution problem directly.

**What do they contribute?**
Product specifications, installation requirements, warranty documentation, expected service intervals, recall notices, and end-of-life guidance. These records complete the component-level layer of the Institutional Record and provide the Registry with authoritative data for maintenance recommendations and system age tracking.

**What do they receive?**
A permanent, property-anchored channel for warranty registration, recall notification, and product lifecycle communication that reaches every subsequent owner of a property containing their product. Manufacturers currently lose contact with consumers at the point of resale. The Registry eliminates that loss.

**What responsibilities do they carry?**
Manufacturers carry the responsibility of maintaining accurate, current, and complete product records — including timely recall notices. A manufacturer whose recall data is absent from the Institutional Record may expose the Registry to reputational risk if a property owner suffers harm from an unnoticed recall of a documented product.

**What dependencies exist?**
The Registry depends on manufacturer participation for component-level documentation that cannot be sourced elsewhere at scale. Manufacturers depend on the Registry — over time — as their most reliable channel for post-sale product communication in the residential market.

**Where are incentives aligned or in conflict?**
Aligned: manufacturers benefit from persistent product documentation and a direct channel to subsequent owners. The Registry benefits from authoritative component records. In conflict: manufacturers may initially resist integration if they perceive the Registry as a competitive data aggregator rather than a distribution partner. Early partnership framing must emphasize the Registry as infrastructure, not a product competitor.

**What risks emerge if the relationship fails?**
Without manufacturer participation, the Institutional Record lacks authoritative component documentation. System ages, warranty status, and recall exposure must be inferred from homeowner records rather than manufacturer data — a materially lower confidence level. The Registry's maintenance recommendation capability is weakened, and the Registry's value to insurance carriers and lenders who rely on system age data is reduced.

### Property Managers

**Strategic Importance:** Important

Property managers oversee residential properties on behalf of owners — maintaining records of maintenance, repairs, tenant activity, and system servicing across the management period. In rental and investment property contexts, property managers are often the most consistent record-keepers for a property across multiple tenancy cycles.

**What is their relationship to Haus Registry?**
Property managers are longitudinal record custodians for properties they manage. Their maintenance logs, service records, and repair histories are directly relevant to the Institutional Record — particularly for properties that have been rental or investment assets before becoming owner-occupied.

**Why does that relationship exist?**
Properties managed by professional managers often have better maintenance records than owner-occupied properties — because property managers operate systematically and maintain records for liability and reporting purposes. That record quality is an asset to the Institutional Record if it can be captured.

**What do they contribute?**
Maintenance logs, service records, repair histories, vendor documentation, and system servicing records accumulated during the management period. For properties with long rental histories, this documentation fills gaps that would otherwise be invisible to incoming owner-stewards.

**What do they receive?**
A structured record system that reduces their administrative burden, supports handoff documentation when management changes, and creates a verifiable maintenance history that supports property valuations and owner reporting.

**What responsibilities do they carry?**
Property managers carry the responsibility of accurate and complete record-keeping during their management period. Records submitted to the Institutional Record during a management period become part of the property's permanent history — accessible to future stewards regardless of management continuity.

**What dependencies exist?**
The Registry depends on property manager participation to capture records for a property category — rental and investment properties — that is otherwise underrepresented in the Institutional Record. Property managers depend on the Registry to provide a durable record system that survives management transitions and ownership changes.

**Where are incentives aligned or in conflict?**
Aligned: property managers benefit from structured, accessible record systems. The Registry benefits from their documentation discipline. In conflict: property managers may resist record submission if they perceive their maintenance records as proprietary or competitively sensitive.

**What risks emerge if the relationship fails?**
Properties with significant rental or investment histories arrive in owner-occupancy with thin or absent Institutional Records. The Registry's coverage of this property segment is materially reduced, and incoming owner-stewards begin their stewardship with less documented history than properties with prior owner-occupancy.

## Part III — Decision-Making Institutions

Decision-Making Institutions rely on the Institutional Record to reduce uncertainty and make consequential decisions about property value, risk, and financing. Their decisions affect whether properties can be bought, sold, insured, and financed — and at what cost. The quality of the Institutional Record directly affects the quality of their decisions.

---

### Mortgage Lenders

**Strategic Importance:** Critical

Mortgage lenders make financing decisions based on property value, condition, and risk. Their underwriting models rely on appraised values, inspection findings, and condition disclosures — all of which are more accurate and reliable when grounded in a complete Institutional Record. A lender financing a property with a strong, verified Institutional Record is underwriting with materially better information than one financing a property whose history is unknown.

**What is their relationship to Haus Registry?**
Lenders are institutional consumers of property condition and history data. The Registry provides a verified, structured source of property documentation that improves underwriting accuracy and reduces the information asymmetries that create lending risk.

**Why does that relationship exist?**
Lenders price risk. A property with an unknown condition history carries more uncertainty — and therefore more risk — than one with a documented, verified record. The Registry reduces that uncertainty, which has direct value to lenders whose risk models depend on property condition accuracy.

**What do they contribute?**
Lenders contribute transaction-level verification events — appraisal records, title searches, and condition assessments that occur at the point of financing. These are significant record events for the Institutional Record, documenting a formal, third-party evaluation of the property at a specific point in time.

**What do they receive?**
Reduced underwriting uncertainty, more accurate collateral assessment, and lower risk of post-close condition surprises on properties with verified Institutional Records. Over time, lenders may develop differentiated underwriting models that reward properties with strong Registry Confidence Scores.

**What responsibilities do they carry?**
Lenders carry the responsibility of submitting appraisal and condition documentation to the Institutional Record as a condition of Registry integration. Their records — particularly appraisal reports — are high-value additions to the property's longitudinal history.

**What dependencies exist?**
The Registry depends on lender adoption to establish the Registry Confidence Score as a recognized input to underwriting models. Lenders depend on the Registry — over time — as a reliable, standardized source of property condition data that reduces the cost and uncertainty of due diligence.

**Where are incentives aligned or in conflict?**
Aligned: both parties benefit from more accurate property condition data. Lenders reduce risk; the Registry increases its value as a data source. In conflict: lenders operate within heavily regulated frameworks that govern what data sources can be used in underwriting decisions. Regulatory acceptance of the Registry Confidence Score as a recognized input is a prerequisite for deep lender integration — and that acceptance must be earned over time.

**What risks emerge if the relationship fails?**
Without lender adoption, the Registry's value in the financing context is limited to the homeowner and transaction participants. The Registry Confidence Score does not become an underwriting input, and the financial incentive for homeowners to maintain strong records — lower financing costs — does not materialize. This limits the Registry's ability to drive record-keeping behavior through economic incentives.

---

### Insurance Carriers

**Strategic Importance:** Critical

Insurance carriers price homeowner risk based on property condition, system ages, construction quality, and loss history. Their underwriting models carry significant uncertainty because the information they rely on is often self-reported, unverified, or incomplete. A property with a verified Institutional Record — documented system ages, maintenance history, and professional condition assessments — is a fundamentally different underwriting proposition than one whose history is unknown.

**What is their relationship to Haus Registry?**
Insurance carriers are consequential consumers of property condition data. The Registry provides verified, structured property documentation that improves risk assessment accuracy and reduces the information asymmetries that lead to mispriced premiums and unexpected claims.

**Why does that relationship exist?**
Insurers price risk they cannot directly observe. A homeowner's representation of their property's condition is the primary input to underwriting — and it is unverified. The Registry replaces self-reported condition with documented, verified history, which is materially more accurate and more defensible.

**What do they contribute?**
Claims records — one of the most consequential record types for the Institutional Record. A documented claim, repair, and resolution creates a verified condition history that is directly relevant to future owners, buyers, and subsequent insurers. Carrier-submitted claims records are high-confidence additions to the Institutional Record.

**What do they receive?**
More accurate risk assessment, reduced claims uncertainty, and the ability to differentiate pricing between properties with strong Institutional Records and those without. Over time, carriers who integrate Registry data into underwriting models may achieve measurable loss ratio improvements on Registry-verified properties.

**What responsibilities do they carry?**
Carriers carry the responsibility of submitting claims records to the Institutional Record as a condition of Registry integration. Claims that are documented, repaired, and verified through the Registry provide future stewards with complete condition history — including events that might otherwise be concealed at transaction time.

**What dependencies exist?**
The Registry depends on carrier integration to capture claims records — a record type that cannot be sourced from any other participant. Carriers depend on the Registry — over time — as a verified data source that reduces underwriting uncertainty across a property's ownership history.

**Where are incentives aligned or in conflict?**
Aligned: both parties benefit from accurate property condition data. Carriers reduce loss uncertainty; the Registry gains access to claims records that dramatically increase the Institutional Record's evidentiary completeness. In conflict: carriers may initially resist sharing claims data — particularly on properties with adverse loss histories — if they perceive disclosure as commercially disadvantageous. The Registry's value proposition must make contribution clearly beneficial before claims data flows reliably.

**What risks emerge if the relationship fails?**
Without carrier integration, claims records are absent from the Institutional Record. Properties with significant loss histories — water damage, fire, structural events — may arrive at transaction time with no documented evidence of prior conditions. This is among the most significant information asymmetries in residential real estate, and the Registry cannot resolve it without carrier participation.

### Appraisers

**Strategic Importance:** Important

Appraisers produce the formal valuations that underpin financing decisions and tax assessments. Their valuations depend on accurate information about a property's condition, improvement history, and comparable sales — all of which are more reliable when grounded in a verified Institutional Record.

**What is their relationship to Haus Registry?**
Appraisers are professional consumers of property condition and improvement data. A complete Institutional Record — including documented improvements, permit history, and system ages — reduces the appraiser's information gap and produces more accurate, defensible valuations.

**Why does that relationship exist?**
Appraisers currently rely on visual inspection, seller-provided information, and comparable sales data to produce valuations. The Institutional Record provides a third input — verified property history — that reduces reliance on self-reported information and improves the accuracy of condition adjustments.

**What do they contribute?**
Formal valuation records that represent a professional, credentialed assessment of the property's market value at a specific point in time. Appraisal records submitted to the Institutional Record become high-value longitudinal data points — documenting not just condition but market value at each assessment.

**What do they receive?**
Faster, more complete access to property history before conducting inspections. A verified Institutional Record reduces the appraiser's due diligence burden and supports more defensible valuation adjustments for documented improvements.

**What responsibilities do they carry?**
Appraisers carry the professional responsibility of accurate, unbiased valuation. Records submitted to the Institutional Record through Registry Pro carry the appraiser's professional credential and become a permanent part of the property's documented history.

**What dependencies exist?**
The Registry depends on appraiser participation to capture formal valuation records. Appraisers depend on the Registry — over time — to reduce the information asymmetry they currently manage through manual investigation.

**Where are incentives aligned or in conflict?**
Aligned: both parties benefit from more complete property information. Appraisers produce better valuations; the Registry gains authoritative valuation records. In conflict: appraisers operate within regulatory frameworks that govern their independence. Any perception that the Registry influences valuation — rather than simply providing information — creates professional liability concerns that must be carefully managed.

**What risks emerge if the relationship fails?**
Without appraiser participation, formal valuation records are absent from the Institutional Record. The Registry's evidentiary depth at transaction time is reduced, and the longitudinal value history of the property — one of the most useful datasets for future market analysis — is not captured.

### Institutional Investors

**Strategic Importance:** Opportunistic

Institutional investors — including single-family rental operators, real estate investment trusts, and portfolio acquisition firms — own and manage residential properties at scale. Their operational and acquisition decisions depend on property condition data that is rarely available in structured, verified form at the portfolio level.

**What is their relationship to Haus Registry?**
Institutional investors are high-volume consumers of property condition and history data. A Registry that provides verified, structured Institutional Records across large property portfolios reduces the due diligence cost and condition uncertainty that currently makes portfolio acquisition and management operationally expensive.

**Why does that relationship exist?**
Institutional investors make acquisition and capital allocation decisions across hundreds or thousands of properties. The information asymmetries that affect individual buyers affect institutional buyers at scale — amplified by the volume of decisions and the cost of due diligence errors. Verified Institutional Records reduce that cost.

**What do they contribute?**
Maintenance records, capital improvement documentation, and condition histories accumulated during institutional ownership periods. Properties held by institutional investors are often better maintained and more systematically documented than owner-occupied properties — their records are a valuable addition to the Institutional Record.

**What do they receive?**
Structured, verified property condition data at portfolio scale, reducing due diligence cost and condition uncertainty in acquisition and disposition decisions.

**What responsibilities do they carry?**
Institutional investors carry the responsibility of submitting maintenance and improvement records during their ownership period — ensuring that properties exiting institutional ownership carry strong Institutional Records for incoming owner-stewards.

**What dependencies exist?**
The Registry depends on institutional investor participation to access a property segment with high documentation discipline and significant portfolio scale. Institutional investors depend on the Registry — over time — as a standardized data source that reduces due diligence cost across portfolio transactions.

**Where are incentives aligned or in conflict?**
Aligned: both parties benefit from structured, verified property data. In conflict: institutional investors may resist record submission for properties with adverse condition histories that affect disposition value.

**What risks emerge if the relationship fails?**
Limited near-term impact. The institutional investor segment is Opportunistic in the Registry's current phase. Failure to engage this segment does not threaten the Registry's core mission but limits the completeness of the Institutional Record for a specific property category.

## Part IV — Market Infrastructure Partners

Market Infrastructure Partners influence the Registry's interoperability, distribution, adoption, and integration across the residential property ecosystem. They do not create or consume the Institutional Record directly — they shape the environment in which the Registry operates and the channels through which it reaches participants.

---

### MLS Organizations

**Strategic Importance:** Critical

Multiple Listing Services are the primary data infrastructure of the residential real estate transaction market. They aggregate property listings, transaction history, and market data across participating brokerages. Their data coverage, market penetration, and industry relationships make them among the most consequential infrastructure partners the Registry will engage.

**What is their relationship to Haus Registry?**
MLS organizations are distribution and interoperability partners. A Registry that is integrated with MLS platforms reaches every transaction where MLS data is used — which is the substantial majority of residential transactions in organized markets. MLS integration is the most direct path to transaction-time Registry visibility.

**Why does that relationship exist?**
The Registry's value is most visible at the moment of transaction — when buyers, agents, and lenders need property history and condition documentation. MLS platforms are the infrastructure through which transaction participants access property data. Registry integration with MLS platforms makes the Registry a standard component of the transaction information stack rather than an optional add-on.

**What do they contribute?**
Distribution reach, transaction-time visibility, and data interoperability across participating brokerages. MLS organizations also contribute listing history and transaction records that add to the Institutional Record's longitudinal data.

**What do they receive?**
Enhanced listing data — the ability to surface Registry Confidence Scores and Registry Verified badges alongside MLS listings, differentiating their data offering and providing member agents with better property information.

**What responsibilities do they carry?**
MLS organizations carry the responsibility of accurate, current, and complete listing data. Their data quality directly affects the quality of the Institutional Record's transaction-layer documentation.

**What dependencies exist?**
The Registry depends on MLS integration for transaction-time distribution and listing data. MLS organizations depend on data partners like the Registry to enhance the value of their listing infrastructure as the market increasingly demands verified property information.

**Where are incentives aligned or in conflict?**
Aligned: both parties benefit from richer, more verified property data at the point of transaction. In conflict: MLS organizations are member-governed bodies whose members include brokerages with varying interests in Registry adoption. MLS governance structures can slow integration even when the technical and commercial case is clear.

**What risks emerge if the relationship fails?**
Without MLS integration, the Registry's transaction-time visibility is limited to direct adoption by individual agents and homeowners. Distribution is slower, adoption requires more friction, and the Registry's presence at the most consequential moment of a property's lifecycle — the transaction — is reduced.

---

### Property Data Platforms

**Strategic Importance:** Important

Property data platforms — including automated valuation model providers, property intelligence platforms, and real estate data aggregators — aggregate, analyze, and distribute property data at scale. Their datasets include ownership history, transaction records, tax assessments, and in some cases physical characteristics and condition estimates.

**What is their relationship to Haus Registry?**
Property data platforms are both potential partners and potential competitors. As partners, they represent distribution channels and data enrichment opportunities. As competitors, they may develop verified property record capabilities that overlap with the Registry's value proposition.

**Why does that relationship exist?**
Property data platforms hold large-scale property datasets that could enrich the Institutional Record — particularly for historical ownership, transaction, and assessment data. The Registry holds verified, longitudinal condition records that property data platforms currently lack. The relationship is potentially complementary.

**What do they contribute?**
Historical ownership records, transaction histories, tax assessment data, and in some cases physical characteristic data. These datasets fill gaps in the Institutional Record for properties whose condition history predates Registry adoption.

**What do they receive?**
Access to verified, property-anchored condition records that improve the accuracy and value of their property intelligence offerings.

**What responsibilities do they carry?**
Data accuracy and provenance transparency. Records sourced from property data platforms must be clearly attributed and confidence-weighted in the Institutional Record — their data quality varies significantly and must not be treated as equivalent to primary source documentation.

**What dependencies exist?**
The Registry depends on property data platforms for historical data enrichment. Property data platforms depend on the Registry — over time — for verified condition records that their current datasets cannot provide.

**Where are incentives aligned or in conflict?**
Aligned: data enrichment benefits both parties. In conflict: property data platforms may view the Registry as a competitive threat as the Registry's market position strengthens. Partnership agreements must be structured to protect the Registry's data independence and prevent proprietary Registry data from being absorbed into competitor offerings.

**What risks emerge if the relationship fails?**
The Registry builds its Institutional Records from primary sources rather than data platform enrichment — which is the correct long-term architecture. Failure to partner with property data platforms slows historical record enrichment but does not threaten the Registry's core mission.

### Enterprise Software Providers

**Strategic Importance:** Important

Enterprise software providers — including property management systems, contractor management platforms, inspection software vendors, and real estate transaction management systems — are the operational infrastructure through which Registry participants currently do their work. Integration with these platforms reduces the friction of Registry participation by meeting participants in the systems they already use.

**What is their relationship to Haus Registry?**
Enterprise software providers are integration partners. Registry API integration with their platforms enables contractors, inspectors, property managers, and agents to submit records to the Institutional Record as a byproduct of their existing workflows — without requiring behavior change.

**Why does that relationship exist?**
The Registry's adoption challenge is not awareness — it is friction. Participants who must change their workflow to contribute records will contribute inconsistently. Participants whose existing software submits records automatically will contribute continuously. Enterprise software integration is the mechanism that converts deliberate contribution into incidental contribution at scale.

**What do they contribute?**
Workflow integration that makes Registry participation frictionless for their users. Records generated within their platforms — inspection reports, work orders, maintenance logs, transaction documents — flow directly to the Institutional Record through Registry API integration.

**What do they receive?**
Enhanced product value for their users — the ability to offer permanent, property-anchored record submission as a feature of their platform. Registry integration makes their software more valuable to participants who care about the durability of their records.

**What responsibilities do they carry?**
Data accuracy, format compliance, and integration reliability. Records submitted through enterprise software integrations must meet Registry source and verification standards — the integration partner is responsible for ensuring their data output conforms to Registry API requirements.

**What dependencies exist?**
The Registry depends on enterprise software integration to achieve incidental contribution at scale — the mode of record generation that does not require behavioral change from participants. Enterprise software providers depend on the Registry API to offer permanent record submission as a platform feature.

**Where are incentives aligned or in conflict?**
Aligned: both parties benefit when Registry integration makes their platform more valuable to participants. In conflict: enterprise software providers may resist integration if they perceive the Registry as a data competitor or if integration requires significant development investment without clear near-term commercial return.

**What risks emerge if the relationship fails?**
Without enterprise software integration, Registry participation remains primarily deliberate — requiring active effort from participants. Contribution rates are lower, records are less complete, and the Registry's growth depends on homeowner and professional behavior change rather than systemic workflow integration. This is a slower and less reliable path to a complete Institutional Record.

## Decisions Established

The following architectural decisions are established by this document and should be treated as settled by all downstream documents:

**D-010 — Stakeholder analysis organized by institutional relationship, not commercial priority**
Stakeholders are grouped by their enduring relationship to Haus Registry and the Institutional Record — Foundational Institutions, Record Ecosystem Partners, Decision-Making Institutions, and Market Infrastructure Partners. Commercial priority, current dependency, or go-to-market sequencing does not govern this organization. That separation ensures the document remains useful across the full lifecycle of the company.

**D-011 — Participant analysis and stakeholder analysis are distinct analytical layers**
Doc 07 defines how participants interact with the Institutional Record. Doc 08 defines institutional relationships with Haus Registry. Entities that appear in both documents are analyzed from different perspectives, not described twice. This convention applies throughout the repository: reappearance signals a change in analytical perspective, not redundancy.

**D-012 — Strategic Importance classifications are property-centric, not revenue-centric**
Foundational, Critical, Important, and Opportunistic classifications reflect each stakeholder's proximity to the property's Institutional Record and the Registry's core mission — not their near-term revenue potential or current go-to-market priority. A stakeholder classified as Foundational may generate no direct revenue. A stakeholder classified as Opportunistic may eventually represent significant commercial value. The classifications are mission-anchored, not financial projections.

**D-013 — Constitutional Traceability, Related Documents, and Downstream Dependencies are repository standards**
Every strategic and constitutional document in this repository should conclude with these three sections. They transform a collection of independent documents into a navigable system — each document knowing what it depends on and what depends on it.

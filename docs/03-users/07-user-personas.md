## Organizing Principle

This document does not describe users.

It describes participants in a property's Institutional Record — the persistent, verified accumulation of information that defines what a property is, what has happened to it, and what can be known about it.

The property is the primary entity. The Institutional Record is the layer through which every participant interacts with that property. No participant owns the record. No transaction exhausts it. No transfer of ownership erases it. The record exists for the property, and it persists across every steward, every transaction, and every generation of ownership.

This distinction governs the organization of every section that follows.

The homeowner is not the most important user. The homeowner is the Primary Steward — the participant whose relationship to the Institutional Record is longitudinal, continuous, and irreplaceable. Every other participant interacts with the record at a specific moment in the property's lifecycle: contributing to it, consuming from it, or helping strengthen it. None of them serves the same function as the steward.

That hierarchy is visible in the depth and structure of what follows.

---

## Part I — Primary Steward

### Homeowner

> *The homeowner is not a user with a profile. The homeowner is the custodian of a property's institutional memory — the only participant whose relationship to the Institutional Record spans years, decisions, and ownership transitions.*

---

#### Who They Are

The homeowner is anyone who holds long-term responsibility for a residential property. They are not defined by demographics. They are defined by their relationship to the property: they live in it, maintain it, invest in it, and are accountable for its condition across time.

They are not always sophisticated. They are not always organized. They are rarely trained in record-keeping. But they are the only participant present for every season of the property's life — every repair, every improvement, every system failure, every permit, every insurance claim. No other participant accumulates more knowledge about the property. And yet, without The Registry, that knowledge evaporates when they leave.

The homeowner is, by necessity and by doctrine, the Primary Steward of the property's Institutional Record.

---

#### Behavioral Portrait

The homeowner does not think in records. They think in problems. The furnace stops working and they call someone. The roof leaks and they find a contractor. The water heater is making noise and they ignore it until it fails. Their relationship with property information is reactive — triggered by malfunction, transaction pressure, or insurance demand — not proactive.

When they do try to organize property information, they reach for what is available: a kitchen drawer, a folder on their desktop, a photo album on their phone. These are not systems. They are accumulations. They have no structure, no verification, no continuity, and no transferability.

When they sell, they discover the limits of this approach. Buyers ask questions they cannot answer. Agents request documentation they cannot produce. Inspectors find conditions they cannot explain. The accumulated knowledge of years of ownership — the contractor who replaced the roof, the permit pulled for the addition, the warranty on the HVAC — is either missing, scattered, or undocumented. The cost of that gap is real: negotiating leverage, buyer confidence, and transaction certainty all diminish when the Institutional Record is absent.

---

#### Behavioral Triggers

The homeowner's engagement with The Registry is event-driven. They are not intrinsically motivated to maintain records. They are motivated by moments that make the absence of records painful.

**Initial triggers** — what brings them to The Registry in the first place:

- A home purchase, where they receive little to no documentation about the property's history
- A major repair or renovation, where they wish they had a record of prior work
- An insurance claim, where they cannot prove the condition or age of a damaged system
- A conversation with a neighbor, agent, or contractor who references the product
- A sale or refinancing process where documentation gaps become visible and costly

**Ongoing triggers** — what sustains their engagement over time:

- Completing a renovation and wanting to capture it before the details fade
- Receiving a warranty that they know they should store somewhere permanent
- A system approaching the end of its expected lifespan, prompting a maintenance review
- Preparing to sell and recognizing that the record they have built has tangible value
- A contractor asking about prior work and being able to answer with documentation

---

#### Frustration Portrait

Without The Registry, the homeowner operates in chronic informational deficit. This deficit is not always visible to them — it becomes acute only in moments of pressure.

They cannot answer basic questions about their own property. When was the roof replaced? What brand is the HVAC? When was the water heater installed? Who did the electrical work? Is the addition permitted? What is under the flooring in the primary bedroom? These are not obscure questions. They are the routine questions of ownership, and the homeowner routinely cannot answer them.

They lose information constantly and invisibly. The contractor's card goes in a drawer and is never seen again. The permit is filed somewhere and cannot be located when the inspector asks for it. The warranty is on a piece of paper that was last seen three moves ago. The appliance manuals are in a binder that has been relocated to the garage. None of this feels like a crisis until the moment it becomes one.

They are vulnerable at transaction time. A buyer's inspector finds evidence of prior water intrusion. The homeowner knows it was addressed, knows the contractor who fixed it, remembers paying for it — but cannot prove any of it. That inability to prove translates directly into negotiating leverage, price concessions, and transaction friction. The knowledge existed. The record did not.

They inherit nothing from prior owners. When they bought the property, they received a disclosure form and a set of appliance manuals, if they were lucky. Everything the prior owner knew about the property — every repair, every improvement, every system decision — was lost at the transfer. They began their stewardship in darkness.

---

#### Goals

- Maintain a complete, accessible record of what has happened to the property under their ownership
- Capture work as it happens rather than reconstructing it later
- Know the current state of every major system without conducting a physical inspection
- Protect themselves against uncertainty at transaction time
- Transfer the property with a stronger Institutional Record than they received
- Access documentation instantly when a contractor, insurer, lender, or buyer requires it

---

#### Trust Expectations

The homeowner's trust in The Registry is earned through reliability, not features. They do not need the Registry to be impressive. They need it to be there — consistent, accurate, and accessible — every time they reach for it.

They expect that what they put in does not disappear. They expect that the record reflects what is true about the property, not what someone guessed or approximated. They expect that when a contractor or buyer looks at the Registry Confidence Score, it reflects the actual quality of the record they have maintained.

They are skeptical of platforms. They have seen apps shut down, companies pivot, and data become inaccessible. Their trust is conditional on permanence. If The Registry cannot credibly claim that it is infrastructure — not a product with a roadmap and an exit strategy — the homeowner will treat it like a product, which means they will treat it as temporary, which means they will not invest in it.

This is why the constitutional commitment to building for decades is not abstract. It is the specific condition under which the homeowner will become a genuine steward rather than a casual user.

---

#### Contribution Patterns

The homeowner contributes to the Institutional Record in two modes: deliberate and incidental.

**Deliberate contribution** happens when the homeowner actively uploads, logs, or documents something they intend to preserve — a completed renovation, a new appliance, a warranty document, a permit. This mode requires friction reduction. The upload must be fast, the classification must be automatic or nearly so, and the confirmation that the record has been captured must be immediate.

**Incidental contribution** happens when the homeowner is doing something else and The Registry captures the information as a byproduct — a contractor completing work through Registry Pro and the record flowing automatically to My Registry, a permit being pulled and Registry AI detecting it, an appliance registration triggering a warranty record. This mode requires no effort from the homeowner. It is the mode that makes the Registry genuinely durable, because it does not depend on the homeowner remembering to document.

Over time, the healthiest Institutional Records will be those where incidental contribution dominates — where the record builds itself as a natural consequence of the property's activity, and the homeowner's deliberate contribution fills the gaps.

---

#### Long-Term Stewardship Behaviors

The homeowner's relationship with The Registry is not a series of transactions. It is a practice that develops over the lifecycle of ownership.

In the early months, they are primarily uploading what they have — prior records, appliance documentation, any history they inherited or can reconstruct. The record is thin and the Registry Confidence Score reflects it.

In the middle years, they develop habits. They take a photo of every completed repair. They ask contractors to log work through Registry Pro. They store warranties in Registry Vault as they receive them. The record thickens.

As a sale approaches, they begin to see the record as an asset. They review it for gaps. They commission inspections they know they can upload. They prepare the Institutional Record for transfer the way a careful owner prepares a property for showing — not because they are required to, but because they understand that the record is now part of what they are selling.

When they transfer the property, they do not leave the new owner in the same darkness they inherited. The record transfers. The stewardship continues. The property is stronger for what they left behind.

---

#### Success Criteria

The homeowner has succeeded as a Primary Steward when:

- They can answer any reasonable question about their property's history without searching drawers or calling contractors
- Their Registry Confidence Score reflects genuine record quality, not just document volume
- The Registry Verified badge accurately represents the verifiable claims in their Institutional Record
- They transfer the property with a materially stronger Institutional Record than they received
- A buyer, agent, or inspector reviewing their Registry Report finds it clear, complete, and credible
- They never experience a transaction where an undocumented condition costs them leverage or certainty

---

#### Registry Habits Across the Ownership Lifecycle

| Phase | Trigger | Registry Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| Purchase | Closing | Upload any records received; begin new ownership layer |
| Early ownership | First repair or renovation | Log work, upload contractor documentation |
| Ongoing | System maintenance | Capture service records, note system ages |
| Mid-ownership | Major improvement | Full documentation: permits, contractor, materials, cost |
| Pre-sale preparation | Decision to sell | Audit record, close gaps, prepare Registry Report |
| Transfer | Closing | Confirm record transfers to incoming steward |

## Part II — Stewardship Transition

### Seller

The seller is not a separate persona. The seller is the Primary Steward at the moment stewardship of the property's Institutional Record transfers to the next owner.

Everything the seller knows about this role, they learned as a homeowner. The frustrations they carry into the transaction are the frustrations of a homeowner who did not have The Registry, or who did not use it as fully as they might have. The advantages they hold are the advantages of a homeowner who built a strong Institutional Record across the years of their ownership.

The seller's relationship to The Registry at the moment of sale is therefore a direct measure of how well they served as a steward.

A seller who maintained a complete Institutional Record enters the transaction in a position of informational strength. They know what was done to the property, when, and by whom. They can prove it. They do not fear the inspector's report because they have documentation for every material condition. They are not negotiating from uncertainty — they are presenting evidence. The Registry Report becomes their instrument of transfer: a clear, verified account of what the property is, what it contains, and what has happened to it under their ownership.

A seller who did not maintain a complete record enters the same transaction from weakness. They know things but cannot prove them. They remember the contractor but not the permit. They know the roof was replaced but not the year or the warranty status. Every undocumented condition becomes a point of negotiation, a source of buyer uncertainty, or a post-inspection concession. The knowledge existed. The record did not. And the cost of that gap is now visible in the transaction.

The seller's role in the final weeks of ownership is to prepare the Institutional Record for transfer. This means closing gaps where possible — commissioning a pre-listing inspection and uploading the result, locating documentation for major systems, capturing any work completed during the sale preparation period. It means reviewing the Registry Confidence Score and understanding what it reflects about the quality of the record. And it means ensuring that the incoming steward receives not just the property but the accumulated institutional knowledge that makes the property knowable.

The goal is to leave the property in a stronger informational state than it was received. This is not merely a courtesy to the buyer. It is the fulfillment of the stewardship responsibility the seller accepted at purchase.

When the transfer is complete, the seller's layer of the Institutional Record does not disappear. It becomes a permanent part of the property's history — a record of what happened under their ownership, preserved for every steward who follows.

## Part III — Incoming Steward

### Buyer

The buyer is not purchasing a property. The buyer is accepting stewardship of a property — and with it, responsibility for the Institutional Record that documents what that property is and what has happened to it.

This distinction changes what the buyer needs from The Registry. A transactional participant needs information sufficient to make a purchase decision. An incoming steward needs something more: confidence that the record they are inheriting is complete, verified, and trustworthy. They need to understand not just what the property contains but how well it is known — the difference between a property with a strong Institutional Record and one whose history is opaque.

The Registry's role in the buyer's experience is to transfer confidence alongside the deed.

---

#### Incoming Steward Profile

**Objectives**

- Understand the property's condition and history before committing to purchase
- Evaluate the completeness and credibility of the Institutional Record
- Identify material conditions that are undocumented or unverified
- Accept a record that reduces uncertainty at the start of their stewardship
- Begin building their own layer of the Institutional Record from day one

**Registry Expectations**

- Access to a Registry Report that is clear, organized, and credible
- A Registry Confidence Score that accurately reflects record quality, not just volume
- Visibility into what is verified, what is asserted, and what is absent
- A record structure that is ready for them to continue building from the point of transfer

**Information Consumed**

- System ages, service histories, and warranty status for major components
- Permit history for improvements and additions
- Contractor and vendor records for prior work
- Inspection reports and documented conditions
- Registry Confidence Score and Registry Verified status

**Decisions Supported**

- Whether to proceed with purchase at the offered price
- What conditions to raise in negotiation based on record gaps
- What inspections to prioritize based on system age and documentation status
- What to budget for deferred maintenance or system replacement
- Where to begin their own stewardship record

**Trust Signals**

- Registry Verified badge indicating third-party verification of key claims
- Source attribution showing where each record originated
- Registry Confidence Score explaining coverage, verification depth, and recency
- Inspection reports and permits as primary source documentation

**Stewardship Begins**

At closing, the buyer's role changes. They are no longer evaluating the record — they are responsible for it. Their first act as Primary Steward should be to review what they have received, identify gaps they intend to close, and begin adding their own layer to the property's Institutional Record. The Registry makes this transition explicit: the incoming steward inherits a record with history, and their ownership adds to it rather than replacing it.

## Part IV — Institutional Record Relationships

The participants in this section are not personas. They are relationships to the property's Institutional Record. Each of them interacts with the record at specific moments in a property's lifecycle — contributing to it, consuming from it, or helping to strengthen it. None of them serves the function of the Primary Steward. All of them are essential to the health and completeness of the Institutional Record.

---

### Real Estate Agent

**Why do they interact with the Registry?**

The agent's professional function is to facilitate the transfer of a property between a seller and a buyer. That transfer is made easier — and more defensible — when the property has a strong, documented Institutional Record. The agent interacts with The Registry because the quality of the record directly affects the quality of the transaction they can deliver: the pricing confidence, the negotiating position, the buyer's certainty, and the speed of close.

**What records do they contribute?**

The agent contributes transactional records — listing documentation, comparative market analysis, offer and acceptance records, and transaction timelines. In some cases they surface records that exist elsewhere (disclosures, prior inspection reports) and ensure they are uploaded to the Institutional Record. Their contribution is often curatorial rather than generative: they identify gaps, request documentation from the homeowner, and facilitate the completion of the record before it is presented to buyers.

**What records do they consume?**

The agent consumes the Registry Report as a transaction instrument. They use the Registry Confidence Score to assess how well-documented the property is relative to comparable listings. They reference permit history and system documentation when preparing the seller's disclosure. They rely on the Institutional Record to answer buyer and inspector questions with evidence rather than assertion.

**What decisions does the Registry support?**

- Pricing strategy, informed by the documented improvement history and system ages
- Disclosure preparation, grounded in verified record rather than seller memory
- Negotiation posture, strengthened by documentation of conditions the buyer raises
- Marketing differentiation, where a Registry Verified property can be presented with demonstrable informational confidence

**What friction exists today?**

The agent currently manages information from multiple fragmented sources — seller disclosures that are incomplete, inspection reports that arrive late, permit records that must be manually requested, and appliance documentation that is missing or approximate. They spend professional time reconstructing information that should already exist. When conditions arise during inspection that the seller believes were previously addressed, the agent has no way to verify the claim. Every undocumented condition becomes a negotiation rather than a resolution.

**How does the Registry reduce that friction?**

The Registry provides the agent with a single, organized record of the property's documented history before the transaction begins. Disclosures can be prepared from verified records rather than seller recollection. Inspection findings can be cross-referenced against documented conditions. Buyer questions can be answered with evidence. The Registry Report becomes a professional instrument the agent presents alongside the listing — not a reactive document assembled under transaction pressure.

**What stewardship responsibility do they have for strengthening the Institutional Record?**

The agent's stewardship responsibility is to ensure that every transaction they facilitate results in a stronger Institutional Record than the one they encountered. This means identifying gaps before listing, not during inspection. It means encouraging sellers to close documentation gaps as part of sale preparation. It means ensuring that the transactional records generated during the sale — disclosures, inspection reports, final documentation — are uploaded to the record and transferred to the incoming steward. An agent who treats the Registry as infrastructure rather than paperwork leaves every property they touch in a stronger informational state than they found it.

---

### Inspector

**Why do they interact with the Registry?**

The inspector's function is to assess the current condition of a property at a specific moment in time. That assessment is more accurate and more useful when it is grounded in the property's documented history. The inspector interacts with The Registry because prior records — system ages, permit history, prior inspection findings, contractor documentation — inform what they look for, what they find, and how they contextualize what they see.

**What records do they contribute?**

The inspector contributes among the most durable records in the Institutional Record: a dated, professional assessment of the property's condition at a specific point in time. An inspection report attached to the Institutional Record becomes a verified baseline — a reference point against which future conditions can be compared, future claims can be evaluated, and future stewards can understand what was known and when. This record type carries high evidentiary value and substantially increases the Registry Confidence Score.

**What records do they consume?**

The inspector consumes system age records, permit history, prior inspection reports, and contractor documentation. Knowing when the HVAC was last serviced, whether an addition was permitted, or what a prior inspector noted about the foundation allows the current inspector to contextualize their findings rather than evaluate the property in isolation. A prior inspection report does not constrain their professional judgment — it provides historical context that makes that judgment more precise.

**What decisions does the Registry support?**

- Prioritization of inspection focus based on system ages and prior documented conditions
- Contextualization of findings against prior reports and known repair history
- More accurate assessment of deferred versus addressed maintenance
- Communication of findings to buyers and sellers grounded in documented history rather than conjecture

**What friction exists today?**

The inspector typically arrives at a property knowing very little about it. They conduct their assessment in isolation from the property's documented history — not because that history is irrelevant, but because it does not exist in accessible form. They cannot know whether the condition they observe is new or recurring, addressed or ignored, permitted or unpermitted, without conducting their own investigation. They produce a report that is accurate to the moment but disconnected from the record of what came before.

**How does the Registry reduce that friction?**

The Registry gives the inspector documented history before they arrive. System ages, prior inspection findings, permit records, and contractor documentation are available through Registry Pro at the time of engagement. The inspector can calibrate their focus before they step onto the property and contextualize their findings against what was previously known. Their report becomes not just an assessment but an addition to the property's longitudinal record — a new layer of verified documentation that increases the accuracy and completeness of the Institutional Record.

**What stewardship responsibility do they have for strengthening the Institutional Record?**

The inspector's report is one of the highest-value records an Institutional Record can contain — professional, dated, and condition-specific. Their stewardship responsibility is to ensure that report becomes part of the permanent record rather than a transaction document that is filed and forgotten. Inspectors who submit reports directly to the Institutional Record through Registry Pro contribute evidence that strengthens the record for every future participant: the buyer who inherits the property, the agent who eventually lists it, the contractor who is asked to address a condition, the insurer who evaluates a claim. A single inspection report, permanently attached to the Institutional Record, serves the property for decades.

---

### Contractor

**Why do they interact with the Registry?**

The contractor performs work on the property. That work changes the property's condition, its systems, its documented history, and its value. The contractor interacts with The Registry because documenting completed work creates a verifiable record that protects them professionally, demonstrates the quality of their work, and strengthens the Institutional Record that their client is building.

**What records do they contribute?**

The contractor contributes the most granular and specific records in the Institutional Record: the scope of work performed, the materials used, the systems affected, the permits pulled, the warranties issued, and the date of completion. These records are irreplaceable. No other participant has direct knowledge of what was done, how it was done, and what was used. When a contractor documents their work through Registry Pro, they create a record that survives the transaction, survives the ownership change, and serves as permanent evidence of what was done and when.

**What records do they consume?**

The contractor consumes prior work records for the systems they are engaging with — not because they cannot assess current conditions, but because prior documentation accelerates their assessment and prevents redundant work. Knowing that the HVAC was serviced eighteen months ago, knowing that the electrical panel was upgraded under permit, knowing that the waterproofing on the foundation was addressed four years earlier — all of this is directly relevant to the scope, the approach, and the pricing of new work. A contractor who arrives with access to the property's documented history is more efficient and more accurate than one who must reconstruct context from observation alone.

**What decisions does the Registry support?**

- Scoping work accurately based on documented system history rather than visual assessment alone
- Recommending appropriate maintenance or repair based on age and service record
- Pricing work with confidence when prior work is documented and verifiable
- Avoiding redundant work or incorrect assumptions about what has been previously addressed
- Issuing warranties with accurate start dates grounded in documented installation records

**What friction exists today?**

The contractor typically arrives without access to the property's history. They assess current conditions, ask the homeowner what they know, and proceed on the basis of what they can observe and what they are told. When prior work was done by another contractor, they may not know what approach was taken, what materials were used, or what conditions existed at the time. They issue warranties without knowing whether the underlying systems were properly installed or previously compromised. When their work is done, the documentation they provide — the invoice, the warranty card, the permit number — goes into the homeowner's possession and is frequently lost within a year.

**How does the Registry reduce that friction?**

The Registry provides the contractor with documented property history through Registry Pro before they begin work. Prior work records, system ages, permit history, and relevant documentation are accessible at the point of engagement. When work is complete, the contractor submits documentation directly — scope, materials, warranty, permit — and the record attaches permanently to the property's Institutional Record rather than to the homeowner's paper filing system. The contractor's professional record follows the work, not the client.

**What stewardship responsibility do they have for strengthening the Institutional Record?**

The contractor is among the most important contributors to a property's Institutional Record. Their work changes the property in ways that no other participant can fully document. Their stewardship responsibility is to treat documentation as a professional standard rather than an administrative burden — to ensure that every material change they make to a property is captured in a form that serves the property for as long as that property stands. A contractor who consistently documents their work through Registry Pro builds a verifiable professional record that benefits their clients, strengthens the properties they work on, and demonstrates the quality of their craftsmanship to every future participant who reviews the Institutional Record.

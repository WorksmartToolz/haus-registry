Document 02 established the market failure: residential real estate lacks a persistent
institutional record of the property's physical lifecycle. Records are continuously
created and continuously abandoned. Every ownership transition severs the threads
connecting a property to its history.

This document does not restate that failure. It derives from it.

Every belief stated here follows directly from the structural reality Document 02
describes. Every product decision, engineering decision, partnership decision, and
business decision at Haus Registry should be traceable to one or more of these beliefs.

## The Doctrine

### I. The Property Is the Primary Entity

Every system that exists today organizes property information around participants —
the owner, the contractor, the insurer, the municipality, the lender. The property
is a reference object in each of these systems. It is not the primary entity.

We believe this is the root architectural error from which every other inefficiency
follows. When information is organized around participants rather than properties,
it disperses when participants move on. When it is organized around the property,
it persists.

**This belief governs every technical decision we make.**

The Registry ID is not a database convenience. It is a permanent identifier that
belongs to the property — independent of every owner, every address change, every
legal boundary modification, every contractor relationship. Everything we build
attaches to this identifier. Nothing we build attaches primarily to a person.

When ownership changes, the record continues. The new owner does not inherit an
empty file. They inherit a property with a history — and the obligation to continue it.

This principle is non-negotiable. Any system, feature, or partnership that requires
treating the owner as the primary entity reproduces the market failure we exist
to solve. We will not build it.

---

### II. Records Must Accumulate, Not Reset

The residential real estate market treats every ownership transfer as a new beginning.
Prior records disperse. New owners start over. The cycle repeats.

We believe this cycle is not inevitable. It is a consequence of infrastructure that
was never designed to persist.

A roof replacement documented today is more valuable five years from now than it
is today. An inspection report filed at closing is more valuable to the next buyer
than it was to the current one. A permit pulled for a kitchen renovation is more
valuable when the home sells than when the work is done.

The value of a property's records does not depreciate with time. It appreciates —
but only if the records are preserved. Systems that reset at ownership transfer
destroy accumulating value. Systems that accumulate create it.

**We will always build for accumulation.**

Every record added to a Property Registry makes the next record more valuable.
Every year of documented history increases the worth of every prior entry.
Every ownership transition that preserves the record rather than abandoning it
compounds the value of everything that came before.

This is not a product feature. It is a design axiom. Every decision about data
retention, ownership transitions, and record persistence must be evaluated against
this principle.

---

### III. We Do Not Create Knowledge. We Assemble Records.

This distinction is foundational to how Haus Registry understands its role.

Knowledge about every property already exists. It exists in contractor systems,
permit offices, homeowner files, manufacturer databases, insurance records, and
inspection reports. The market does not fail to generate knowledge about its
properties. It fails to assemble that knowledge into a persistent, property-centric
record that any future participant can access and rely on.

We are not in the business of generating facts. We are not appraisers. We are not
inspectors. We are not contractors. We do not determine what is true about a property.

**We assemble what is already known. We preserve it. We connect it. We make it
accessible across ownership cycles.**

This belief has direct consequences for how we describe ourselves, how we price
our product, how we train our AI, how we present data to users, and how we defend
our role in disputes. We never claim to know something we have only collected.
We always show the source of what we hold.

This is the difference between a registry and an oracle. We are a registry.

---

### IV. The Source of Every Record Must Be Visible

Trust cannot be manufactured. It can only be earned — record by record, source
by source, verification by verification.

We believe that transparency about the origin of every record is not a disclosure
requirement. It is the product. A record whose source is visible is worth more
than a record whose source is hidden, because it can be evaluated, challenged,
and verified by anyone who relies on it.

**Every record in The Registry displays its source. Always.**

This means a homeowner's claim is labeled as a homeowner's claim. A contractor's
invoice is labeled as a contractor's invoice. A municipal permit is labeled as a
municipal permit. A government record is labeled as a government record. An AI
estimate is labeled as an AI estimate.

We never obscure the origin of information to make it appear more authoritative
than it is. We never aggregate sources in a way that hides their individual
reliability. We never present a derived conclusion without showing what it was
derived from.

This belief will sometimes make our product appear less certain than competitors
who present unverified claims as facts. We accept that tradeoff. A registry that
cannot be trusted is not a registry. It is noise.

---

### V. Verification Increases Value. It Does Not Create Facts.

Not all records carry the same weight. A homeowner's recollection that the roof
was replaced eight years ago is not the same as a contractor's invoice for that
replacement, which is not the same as a permit record for that work, which is not
the same as a permit record linked to a final municipal inspection.

Each layer of verification makes the underlying record more reliable — more useful
to buyers, lenders, insurers, and appraisers who must make decisions based on it.

**We believe verification is a spectrum, not a binary.**

We do not divide the world into verified and unverified. We build a verification
model that reflects the actual reliability of each source, allows every record to
carry its verification level visibly, and enables future participants to evaluate
records according to their own tolerance for uncertainty.

This belief governs how we design the Registry Confidence Score, the Registry
Verified badge, and every presentation of data within the platform. We will never
compress this spectrum into a simple pass/fail signal. The nuance is not a
limitation — it is the product.

Verification is the mechanism by which records become more reliable over time.
It does not change the underlying facts. It changes how much confidence a future
participant can place in those facts.

---

### VI. Confidence Reduces Uncertainty. Reduced Uncertainty Creates Value.

We believe the economic case for Haus Registry is fundamentally a case about
uncertainty reduction.

Every participant in the residential real estate ecosystem — buyers, sellers,
lenders, insurers, appraisers, contractors — operates under informational
uncertainty about the properties they transact with. That uncertainty has a cost.
It is priced into interest rates. It is priced into insurance premiums. It is
priced into appraisal discounts for undocumented improvements. It is priced into
the time and expense of due diligence that reconstructs what was already known.

When a Property Registry exists — when a buyer can access twenty years of verified
maintenance records, when a lender can verify the condition history of their
collateral, when an insurer can assess risk against actual documented claims rather
than actuarial averages — uncertainty decreases. And decreased uncertainty has
measurable economic value for every participant.

**We are not selling documentation. We are selling confidence.**

A Registry Report is not a collection of files. It is a reduction in the uncertainty
that every future participant would otherwise carry. The Registry Confidence Score
is not a rating. It is a quantification of how much uncertainty has been eliminated
about a specific property.

This belief shapes how we price the product, how we communicate its value, and
how we measure whether we are succeeding. The right question is not how many
records a property has. It is how much uncertainty those records eliminate for
the participants who rely on them.

---

### VII. Trust Is the Market Outcome We Are Building Toward

Confidence is what individual participants feel when they access a verified property
record. Trust is what the market develops when verified property records are
consistently available, consistently accurate, and consistently relied upon.

We believe these are different things and both matter.

**Confidence is personal. Trust is institutional.**

A buyer who accesses a Registry Report and finds twenty years of verified records
gains confidence in that specific property. A market in which Registry Reports
are standard components of every transaction — expected by buyers, required by
lenders, reviewed by insurers — develops institutional trust in the Registry
as infrastructure.

We are building toward institutional trust. That means every individual record
must be worth trusting. It means every confidence score must accurately reflect
the reliability of the underlying documentation. It means every time a participant
relies on a Registry Report and finds it accurate, we have built another unit of
the institutional trust we are ultimately creating.

Trust is not a marketing claim. It is a compounding asset built one accurate
record at a time. We take that seriously in every decision about what we collect,
how we verify it, and how we present it.

---

### VIII. The Home Exists for Decades. We Build for Decades.

The average American homeowner holds a property for approximately eight years.
The average American home stands for fifty years or more. Most homes will be
bought and sold multiple times across generations.

We believe that infrastructure designed for any horizon shorter than the property's
lifespan will eventually fail the property and everyone who relies on its record.

**We build for fifty years, not five.**

This belief has consequences that are sometimes uncomfortable in a startup context.
It means we do not optimize for growth metrics at the expense of data integrity.
It means we do not deprecate records to save storage costs. It means we version
every edit rather than allowing overwrites. It means we design ownership transition
workflows for the hundredth owner as carefully as for the second.

It also means the decisions we make today — about the Registry ID schema, the
data model, the verification hierarchy, the API contract — will be lived with
for decades by properties, participants, and markets we cannot yet fully anticipate.

We make those decisions accordingly.

---

### IX. We Are Infrastructure. We Do Not Behave Like a Product.

There is a meaningful difference between a company that builds a product and a
company that builds infrastructure. The difference is not scale. It is orientation.

Products are designed around user acquisition, engagement, and retention. They
optimize for the experience of the participant in front of them. Infrastructure
is designed around reliability, consistency, and persistence. It optimizes for
the needs of every participant who will ever rely on it — including participants
who do not yet exist.

We believe Haus Registry is infrastructure. That belief governs how we make
decisions that products do not face.

**We do not sunset records.** A record that exists in a Property Registry must
remain accessible for as long as the property exists. We do not delete history
because it is old, inconvenient, or commercially unimportant to us.

**We do not sell participant data.** The records in The Registry exist to serve
the property and the participants who legitimately need its history. They do not
exist as inventory for advertising, lead generation, or third-party monetization
without explicit participant consent.

**We do not optimize engagement at the expense of accuracy.** We will never
design a feature that encourages participants to add records without verifying
them, inflate confidence scores to increase platform activity, or present
uncertain information as certain to improve user satisfaction metrics.

**We remain neutral among participants.** Buyers and sellers, lenders and
borrowers, insurers and policyholders, contractors and homeowners — all of them
rely on The Registry. We do not take sides. We do not design the platform to
advantage one participant at the expense of another. We provide the record.
Participants make decisions from it.

Infrastructure that behaves like a product eventually becomes neither.

---

### X. Every Decision Is a Precedent

Haus Registry is being built to last. Every technical decision, product decision,
partnership decision, and business decision sets a precedent that will be lived
with — by future engineers, future executives, future partners, and future
participants — for years or decades.

We believe this creates an obligation of deliberateness that most companies do
not face.

**When we make a decision, we document it.** Not as bureaucracy — as institutional
memory. The company that forgets why it made its foundational decisions is the
company that reverses them under pressure and loses the coherence that made it
valuable.

**When we make a trade-off, we name it.** Every design decision involves
trade-offs. We do not pretend otherwise. We state what we chose, what we
did not choose, and why. Future participants in these decisions deserve that
context.

**When we are wrong, we say so.** A registry that cannot acknowledge its own
errors cannot be trusted. The same standard we hold for property records — every
edit is auditable, nothing is overwritten — we hold for our own decisions.

This is not humility as a virtue. It is coherence as a requirement. An
infrastructure company that loses coherence between its stated beliefs and its
actual decisions loses the only thing that makes it infrastructure rather than
just another platform.

---

## What the Doctrine Rules Out

A doctrine is only as useful as the decisions it prevents. The following categories
of decision are ruled out by the beliefs stated above, regardless of their
short-term commercial appeal.

**We will not build owner-centric data models.** Any architecture that makes the
owner the primary entity reproduces the market failure. This rules out approaches
where records disappear when an owner account is closed, where property history
is housed in user profiles, or where ownership transfer requires migrating data
between accounts.

**We will not present unverified claims as verified facts.** Aggregating
homeowner-submitted records without verification levels, displaying confidence
scores that do not reflect actual source reliability, or labeling AI estimates
as documented facts are all prohibited by the doctrine.

**We will not monetize participant data without consent.** Selling lead data to
contractors, providing buyer behavior data to sellers, or offering property
condition information to insurers without homeowner authorization are all
prohibited.

**We will not optimize for engagement at the expense of accuracy.** Features
designed to increase record counts without increasing record quality, gamification
that incentivizes unverified submissions, or confidence score inflation for
retention purposes are all prohibited.

**We will not sunset records for commercial reasons.** Deleting or archiving
records because they are old, because the associated owner has left the platform,
or because they are not commercially useful to us is prohibited.

## The Relationship Between the Doctrine and What Follows

This document defines what Haus Registry believes. It does not define how those
beliefs are implemented.

The Trust Framework (Document 04) translates the beliefs in Sections IV, V, VI,
and VII into a precise operating model: how verification levels are defined, how
the Registry Confidence Score is calculated, how Registry Verified is earned and
displayed, and how the Registry Report is assembled and presented.

The Product Requirements Document translates the beliefs in Sections I, II, III,
and IX into specific features, acceptance criteria, and explicit non-goals.

The Data Architecture and Event Data Model translate the beliefs in Sections I,
II, and VIII into schema decisions, retention policies, and versioning requirements.

Every downstream document should be able to cite a specific section of this
Doctrine as the governing reason for every significant decision it contains.
If a downstream document contains a decision that cannot be traced to this
Doctrine, one of two things is true: either the decision is wrong, or the
Doctrine is incomplete.

Both possibilities should be taken seriously.

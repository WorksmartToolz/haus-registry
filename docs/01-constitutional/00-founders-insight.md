# The Day I Realized Every Home Loses Its Memory

I was looking for a receipt.

Not for anything dramatic. A repair had been done — something with the HVAC, or maybe the roof, I can't remember exactly — and I needed to find the paperwork. The warranty. The name of the contractor. The date it was done. Something that would tell me what had already been taken care of so I didn't have to start from scratch.

I couldn't find it.

Not because I'm disorganized. Not because the work wasn't done. But because there was nowhere for that information to live. It had been filed somewhere — an email, a drawer, a folder on a computer I no longer owned — and it was gone. Eight years of ownership, and the house didn't remember any of it.

That moment stuck with me.

Not because of the frustration of the search. But because of what the search revealed.

---

Here is what I realized:

Every home I have ever lived in, every home any of us has ever owned, carries a history that is constantly being lost.

The previous owners knew things about the house that I will never know. They knew when the water heater was replaced. They knew which contractor re-routed the plumbing after the 2009 freeze. They knew that the northwest corner of the basement had flooded twice — and that it was fixed. They knew all of this. And when they moved out, that knowledge went with them.

I started fresh. The way every buyer starts fresh.

And when I sell this house, the next owner will start fresh too. They will look at the walls and the floors and the ceilings and try to read the history of the house from its surface, the way you'd try to read a book that someone tore most of the pages out of.

This is not a personal problem. This is how the entire market works.

---

Every home is, in a sense, an amnesiac.

It exists for decades. Sometimes for centuries. It weathers storms and renovations and the ordinary accumulation of time. Families live in it. Work is done on it. Systems are installed, serviced, replaced. Events occur — leaks, fires, foundations that shift, roofs that age. All of that history is real. All of it matters. All of it has bearing on the value and condition and safety of the home.

And almost none of it survives.

Not because it was never documented. It was — in invoices and permits and inspection reports and warranty cards and contractor records. Somewhere, in some drawer or database or filing cabinet, most of it exists.

But it exists in pieces. Separated from the property. Attached to people instead of places. And when those people move on, the pieces scatter.

The home forgets.

---

Once I saw this, I couldn't stop seeing it.

I saw it in every real estate transaction I read about — the buyer paying for an inspection that partially repeated an inspection done three years ago. The seller unable to document improvements that were real and valuable. The lender ordering an appraisal to estimate what should already be known.

I saw it in every contractor relationship — the plumber who had to diagnose a system from scratch because no one could tell him what had been done before. The roofer who replaced flashing that had already been replaced, because no one knew.

I saw it in every home insurance claim — the dispute over whether the water damage was pre-existing, with no documentation on either side to settle the question.

The market was not just inefficient. It was repeatedly paying to rediscover information that had already been discovered. Information that should have been permanent was being treated as temporary. The house existed for decades. The knowledge about the house lasted only as long as the owner who held it.

---

Every other long-lived asset of consequence eventually develops a memory.

Aircraft have maintenance logs that follow them for their entire operational life — not because airline passengers demanded it, but because the stakes of not knowing are too high.

Vehicles have service records and VINs. A car's history follows it through every owner.

Corporations have registries. Financial instruments have audit trails. Medical devices have traceability requirements.

Even land has a memory — a chain of title that preserves ownership history across centuries.

The physical life of a home — what was built, what was repaired, what was replaced, what happened — has no equivalent. The legal record of a home is permanent. The lived record is not.

That asymmetry is the gap.

---

The insight is not complicated.

Homes outlive their owners.

The knowledge about a home should outlive its owners too.

Right now it doesn't. And the entire residential real estate market — buyers, sellers, lenders, insurers, inspectors, contractors, appraisers — pays for that absence every single day.

What's needed is not another app that helps homeowners stay organized.

What's needed is infrastructure. A permanent record that belongs to the property, not to any single owner. A record that accumulates history instead of resetting it. A record that every future participant — every buyer, every lender, every inspector, every insurer — can trust.

A memory that the home keeps, regardless of who lives in it.

That is what Haus Registry is building.

Not because it is a good business idea.

Because it is something that should have existed a long time ago.

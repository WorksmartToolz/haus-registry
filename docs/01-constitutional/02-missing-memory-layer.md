## The Foundational Statement

> *A home should outlast the memory of everyone who has ever lived in it.*

This is the sentence at the center of everything Haus Registry builds.

It is not a product vision. It is an observation about a structural failure so pervasive, so embedded in the normal functioning of the residential real estate market, that it has become invisible.

Homes are among the longest-lived assets in the built world. They stand for fifty years, one hundred years, across generations of families and ownership cycles. They accumulate history — construction, maintenance, repair, renovation, damage, recovery — that has direct bearing on their condition, safety, value, and insurability.

That history should persist and compound in value over time.

Instead, it remains fragmented, disconnected, and inaccessible — never assembled into a persistent institutional record that belongs to the property itself and travels with it across every ownership cycle.

This is the foundational market failure that Haus Registry exists to solve.

## 1. Executive Summary

Residential real estate is the largest single asset class in the United States, with an estimated total value exceeding $45 trillion. It is the primary store of wealth for the majority of American households. Yet despite its economic scale, the residential property market operates without one of the most basic components of any mature asset system: a persistent institutional record of each property's physical lifecycle.

Every long-lived asset of consequence eventually develops a mechanism for assembling, preserving, and transferring records across time and across owners. Aircraft carry maintenance logs that follow the airframe for its entire operational life. Vehicles carry service records linked to persistent identifiers across every ownership change. Corporations maintain audited registries. Financial instruments carry verified audit trails. Medical devices have traceability requirements. Even land carries a chain of legal title that preserves ownership history across centuries.

Residential properties are the exception.

A home built in 1987 accumulates decades of physical history — construction, permits, maintenance, repair, renovation, damage, recovery, system replacements, appliance installations, inspections, insurance claims. That history is continuously created. Records of it exist in contractor systems, permit offices, homeowner files, manufacturer databases, insurance records, inspection reports, email inboxes, and filing cabinets across dozens of disconnected systems.

The records are rarely destroyed.

They are simply never assembled into a persistent institutional record that belongs to the property itself. When ownership changes — which happens on average every eight years — the fragmented records of that property become even more fragmented. The threads connecting evidence to context, event to outcome, repair to warranty are severed. The next owner inherits a property whose history exists in pieces no one can locate, in systems no one can access, held by people who no longer have a relationship with the home.

Every major inefficiency in residential real estate — information asymmetry between buyers and sellers, duplicated inspection and due diligence effort, appraisal uncertainty, insurance underwriting gaps, contractor repeated diagnostics, suppressed improvement values — is a predictable consequence of this absent institutional record.

The market does not destroy knowledge about its properties. It continuously creates it — and continuously fails to carry it forward. Every participant, at every ownership transition, pays to rediscover what already existed. That cycle has continued for generations because no participant has had sufficient scope or incentive to build the persistent institutional record that would interrupt it.

## 2. How Every Mature Asset Market Develops a Persistent Record

The pattern is consistent across asset classes and across history. Every long-lived asset of sufficient economic consequence eventually produces an institution dedicated to assembling and preserving records about that asset across time and ownership. The form varies. The principle does not.

**Aircraft** carry FAA-mandated maintenance logs that follow the airframe for its entire operational life. Every inspection, every part replacement, every airworthiness directive is recorded and transferred with the aircraft. The aviation industry developed this infrastructure not because it was convenient — but because the consequences of operating without a persistent record were unacceptable.

**Vehicles** carry VINs that link registered ownership history, recorded accidents, emissions inspections, and service events to a persistent identifier that survives every ownership change. Carfax built a billion-dollar business on one insight: that a car's documented history has independent economic value separate from the vehicle itself — and that buyers will pay to access it.

**Corporations** are registered entities with persistent legal identities, audited financial histories, and documented governance records that survive changes in ownership and leadership. The institutional record of a corporation is among its most valuable intangible assets.

**Financial instruments** carry complete audit trails. Every trade, every settlement, every beneficial ownership change is recorded. The integrity of financial markets depends entirely on the existence of persistent institutional records about the instruments they trade.

**Medical devices** implanted in patients carry traceability requirements that link the device to its manufacturer, manufacturing lot, and implanting physician — because when a device fails or is recalled, the persistent record of that device is a matter of patient safety.

**Land** carries a chain of legal title that, in many jurisdictions, preserves ownership history across centuries. Title registries are among the oldest forms of institutional infrastructure precisely because the economic stakes of unclear ownership demanded persistent records.

Residential real estate has developed legal registries, tax registries, and ownership registries — all of which preserve institutional records about who owns a property and what encumbrances exist.

It has never developed a lifecycle registry — a persistent institutional record of the physical history of the property itself, maintained across owners and across decades.

This is the missing layer.

## 4. First Principles

These principles are constitutional. Every engineering decision, product decision, business decision, and governance decision at Haus Registry should be traceable to one or more of them.

**Homes outlive owners.**
The average American homeowner holds a property for approximately eight years. The average American home stands for fifty years or more. Infrastructure designed around owners rather than properties will always fail to serve the asset across its actual lifespan.

**Records should outlive ownership.**
Every owner generates records about their property through maintenance, repair, renovation, and the ordinary experience of homeownership. Those records rarely disappear entirely — they disperse, becoming inaccessible to anyone who needs them. A market that allows records to disperse at every ownership transition is a market that continuously pays to rediscover what already existed.

**Documentation should accumulate, not reset.**
The value of a property's records is not static. A roof replacement documented today is more valuable ten years from now — when it informs a buyer's decision, a lender's underwriting, or an insurer's pricing — than it is at the moment it is created. Systems designed to reset at ownership transfer destroy this accumulating value. Systems designed to accumulate it create a compounding asset.

**The property — not the owner — is the primary entity.**
This is not a design preference. It is an architectural principle with direct consequences for every system Haus Registry builds: the Registry ID, the database schema, the permissions model, the ownership transition workflow, the version history system, the API contract, and the security architecture. Every system that treats the owner as the primary entity will reproduce the market failure. Every system that treats the property as the primary entity will interrupt it.

**A property's record is an asset of the property itself.**
Every documented repair, every inspection record, every permit, every maintenance event, every warranty creates a record that extends in value well beyond the moment of the event. Records accumulate. Documentation compounds. A property with twenty years of assembled records is more valuable than a physically identical property with none. Today, that value is invisible because the infrastructure to assemble and preserve those records does not exist.

**Institutional records create more efficient markets.**
Markets function more efficiently when participants share access to accurate, complete, and accessible records. The residential real estate market's structural information asymmetries — between buyers and sellers, between lenders and borrowers, between insurers and policyholders — are largely a consequence of the absence of persistent institutional records about individual properties. Assembling those records does not merely improve one transaction. It improves the functioning of the entire market.

## 5. The Primary Entity Principle: Architectural Consequences

"The property — not the owner — is the primary entity" is the single architectural principle from which the largest number of Haus Registry's technical decisions derive.

Its consequences extend across every layer of the system:

**Registry ID:** Every property receives a permanent, unique identifier at the moment it enters The Registry. This identifier does not change when ownership changes, when addresses change, or when legal boundaries are modified. It is the property's persistent identity — the anchor to which all institutional records attach.

**Database Design:** The property is the root entity in every data model. Owners, events, documents, contractors, permits, warranties, and inspections are all related entities. The owner is a role held by a person for a period of time — not the organizing principle of the data.

**Ownership Transitions:** When a property changes hands, the Registry transfers intact. Nothing is deleted. Nothing is reset. The new owner gains access to the property's accumulated institutional record — they do not inherit an empty file.

**Version History:** Every record is versioned. Every edit is auditable. Nothing is overwritten. The institutional record of the property is preserved in a form that can be interrogated, challenged, and supplemented.

**Permissions Model:** What an owner can see, what a buyer can access, what a contractor can contribute, and what a lender can request are all governed by the property's relationship to each participant — not by any individual owner's account.

**API Contract:** Every API endpoint is organized around the Registry ID. External systems integrate with the property's record — not with any individual owner's account.

**Security Architecture:** Access controls are defined at the property level. The property's record persists across owner changes with continuity of access governance.

This principle is not abstract. It is the foundation from which a coherent, consistent, scalable system can be built. Every deviation from it will reproduce, in some form, the market failure this document defines.

## 6. What Is Broken: The Failure to Assemble a Persistent Institutional Record

The failure of the residential real estate market is not that records disappear. Most records about a property continue to exist somewhere — in contractor systems, permit offices, insurance records, homeowner files, manufacturer databases, inspection reports.

The failure is that these records are never assembled into a persistent institutional record that belongs to the property itself.

They are never connected. Never preserved in a form that transfers with ownership. Never made accessible to the participants who will need them in the future. The records exist — institution-specific, transaction-specific, person-specific — and they remain exactly that: held by whoever created them, in systems designed for that institution's purposes, inaccessible to everyone else.

**At construction**, a home enters the world with a specific set of characteristics — materials, dimensions, systems, specifications — documented in building permits, architectural drawings, and contractor records. This documentation exists across multiple systems. It is never assembled into a coherent record that follows the property.

**During ownership**, every maintenance event, every repair, every system replacement generates records about the property. A homeowner who replaces a roof generates an invoice, a warranty registration, and possibly a permit. A contractor generates a work order and job record. A permit office generates an approval record. Three sources of documentation about one event — never connected, never assembled into a persistent property record.

**At the point of sale**, the primary mechanism for record transfer is the seller disclosure form — a document that is legally constrained, incentive-distorted, and structurally limited to what the seller chooses to disclose and can remember. The records accumulated over years of ownership are partially captured, partially summarized, and largely abandoned. What transfers is not an institutional record. It is a snapshot of what one participant recalled at a moment of significant commercial pressure.

**After closing**, the new owner does not inherit an institutional record. They inherit a property whose documentation exists in pieces across contractors who have moved on, insurers whose records are proprietary, municipalities whose databases require manual retrieval, and prior owners who are no longer parties to any relationship. The new owner begins assembling their own partial records — records that will, in turn, be partially abandoned when they sell.

Across fifty years and multiple ownership cycles, a single property continuously generates records that are repeatedly left fragmented, disconnected, and inaccessible — never assembled into the persistent institutional record that would make that documentation useful to every future participant.

## 7. Where Records Live and Why They Fail to Carry Forward

The records of any residential property exist today — distributed, fragmented, and inaccessible — across the following systems. None are designed to communicate with one another. None are organized around the property. All are organized around the institution that operates them.

**County Assessor Offices** hold records of ownership, assessed values, and basic property characteristics. Their mandate is legal and fiscal. They do not hold improvement records, maintenance histories, or condition documentation.

**Municipal Permit Offices** hold records of permitted work — additions, structural modifications, electrical upgrades, plumbing changes, HVAC installations. These records are among the most authoritative that exist about what was built and approved. Access varies dramatically by jurisdiction; many systems remain undigitized; and unpermitted work leaves no record at all.

**MLS Systems** hold transactional records — listing data, photographs, sale prices. These records are transaction-specific and designed to expire with the listing. They are not designed to persist as a property record or accumulate across ownership cycles.

**Title Companies** hold records of ownership history and encumbrances — the legal record of the property, carefully assembled and preserved. They do not hold records of the property's physical history.

**Inspection Companies** produce the most detailed assessments of a property's physical state that exist at any given moment. These records are delivered to one buyer, retained briefly, and never connected to any subsequent event in the property's history.

**Insurance Carriers** hold records of claims history — one of the richest records of a property's physical experience over time. These records are proprietary, fragmented across carriers as policies change, and inaccessible to the market that would most benefit from them.

**Manufacturers** hold records of what products were registered, under what warranty terms, and by whom. These records are associated with the original purchaser rather than the property. When ownership changes, the connection between the product and the property is severed.

**Contractors** hold records of what they installed, repaired, and diagnosed — in internal systems designed for their business operations. When a contractor retires or goes out of business, those records become effectively inaccessible.

**Homeowners** hold whatever records they choose to maintain, in whatever format they prefer, with no standard, no portability, and no mechanism for transfer. The filing cabinet, the email inbox, the cardboard box, and the smartphone photo library are the de facto infrastructure for residential property documentation.

**Mortgage Lenders**, **Warranty Providers**, and **Property Managers** each hold fragments of records relevant to the property's history — none connected to one another, none organized around the property, none designed to persist beyond the relationship that generated them.

The consistent pattern: records exist in abundance. What does not exist is a persistent institutional record — assembled from all of these sources, organized around the property, preserved across ownership changes, and accessible to every participant who will ever have legitimate need of it.

## 8. Who Bears the Cost

The absence of a persistent institutional record imposes specific, material costs on every participant in the residential real estate ecosystem. In each case, the cost is not the absence of records — it is the inability to access and rely on records that exist but have never been assembled into a persistent, property-centric form.

**Homeowners** cannot reliably answer basic questions about their own property — not because the answers don't exist, but because the answers are fragmented across systems they cannot access. They pay in time spent searching for records, in unnecessary service calls for problems already diagnosed, and in value lost at resale when improvements cannot be substantiated.

**Buyers** make the largest financial commitment of their lives without access to the property's accumulated record. Information asymmetry is structural — not because sellers conceal records, but because neither party has access to an assembled institutional record that would resolve it. Buyers routinely discover post-closing conditions not because sellers were dishonest, but because the seller's own records were incomplete.

**Sellers** who have invested in their properties frequently cannot capture that investment in their sale price because they cannot produce an assembled, accessible record of what was done. The market discounts what it cannot verify. Sellers pay the price of absent institutional records in suppressed sale prices and prolonged negotiation.

**Real Estate Agents** spend significant time gathering basic property information that should be immediately accessible. They rely on seller-provided records — unverified, incomplete, and personal — when an institutional record would serve every party more accurately and more fairly.

**Inspectors** assess what is visible at the time of inspection. Without access to prior condition reports or maintenance records, they cannot determine whether a water stain represents an active problem or a resolved one. They begin from whatever records the seller chooses to share — not from a foundation of assembled property history.

**Appraisers** must accept undocumented improvement claims without support or discount them. The systematic undervaluation of undocumented improvements is a rational response to the absence of an assembled property record, not a failure of appraisal methodology.

**Mortgage Lenders** underwrite against collateral whose history they cannot fully access. Residual uncertainty about physical condition and maintenance history is priced into underwriting standards and interest rates — costs ultimately borne by borrowers who would benefit from lenders having access to a persistent property record.

**Insurance Companies** price risk without the property-level records that would most inform them. When claims occur, disputes about pre-loss conditions are disputes about records that were never assembled into a persistent, accessible form.

**Manufacturers** lose contact with their installed products when ownership changes, because product records are associated with the purchaser rather than the property. Recalls go undelivered. Warranties go unclaimed. Safety risks go unaddressed.

**Contractors** repeatedly encounter properties without accessible records of prior work — not because prior work was undocumented, but because the documentation exists in a prior contractor's system, a prior owner's files, or a prior insurance record that no current participant can access.

**Municipalities** issue permits and conduct inspections with no mechanism to verify completion or track downstream consequences — not because the records don't exist elsewhere, but because they were never assembled into an institutional record that could serve a regulatory purpose.

**Property Managers** who inherit portfolios from prior managers also inherit absent institutional records — no maintenance history, no contractor relationships, no system records assembled in one place. They begin the discovery cycle again.

## 9. Quantifying the Cost

The cost of operating without a persistent institutional record in residential real estate is large, chronic, and distributed across so many participants that no single entity has had reason to measure it comprehensively. What follows distinguishes clearly between documented industry data, estimates grounded in available evidence, and qualitative assessments where quantification is not possible.

**Transaction friction (documented):** The National Association of Realtors documents that the average home sale involves 20 to 30 days of due diligence, a significant portion of which consists of attempting to locate and reconstruct records that — in a market with persistent institutional records — would already be assembled and accessible. Industry estimates suggest documentation-related delays add 5 to 10 days to the average transaction timeline. At an average home price of approximately $400,000 and typical carrying costs of 0.5% per month, each additional week of delay costs approximately $500 per transaction. Across 5 million annual transactions, conservative estimates suggest $2.5 billion or more in annual carrying costs attributable to the absence of assembled property records.

**Inspection duplication (estimated):** Approximately 5 million home inspections are conducted annually in the United States at an average cost of $300 to $500. Industry professionals estimate that 20 to 40 percent of inspection time is spent assessing conditions and systems that prior accessible documentation would already have addressed. This represents between $300 million and $1 billion in annual inspection effort partially repeating prior discovery — not because prior records don't exist, but because they were never assembled into an accessible institutional record.

**Improvement value discounting (estimated):** Appraisers consistently apply lower value adjustments to improvements that cannot be substantiated with accessible records. Discounts of 10 to 30 percent relative to documented improvements are consistent with practitioner experience. Against approximately $500 billion in annual home improvement spending, the aggregate value that fails to transfer because records were never assembled into an accessible property record is substantial.

**Insurance claims disputes (qualitative):** The Insurance Information Institute identifies homeowner claims disputes as among the most common sources of insurance litigation. These disputes are frequently disputes about what the property's pre-loss condition was — disputes that an assembled institutional record of the property's history would resolve or prevent.

**Warranty loss (qualitative):** Industry surveys consistently find that a majority of homeowners are unaware of active warranties on systems and appliances in their homes. This is not a failure of memory alone. It is a failure of assembled institutional records — warranty records exist at the manufacturer level but are never connected to the property in a persistent, accessible form.

The honest summary: the cost of operating without a persistent institutional record is enormous, distributed, and largely invisible — embedded so deeply in the normal functioning of the market that participants have absorbed it as an operating assumption rather than recognizing it as a solvable structural problem.

## 10. Why Existing Solutions Do Not Provide a Persistent Institutional Record

The residential real estate market has produced sophisticated tools and platforms. Each addresses a real need within the context of a specific relationship or transaction. None provides a persistent institutional record of the property across its lifecycle.

**MLS Systems** capture transactional records about listings. They are not designed to persist as a property record, accumulate across ownership cycles, or serve participants outside the transaction context.

**Listing Portals** aggregate transactional records for property discovery. They are built around the listing — a temporary event — not the property's accumulated history.

**Title Companies** assemble and preserve the legal record of a property with precision and authority. They do not assemble or preserve the physical record.

**Inspection Reports** produce authoritative records of current conditions. Those records are delivered to one buyer and never connected to prior inspections, maintenance records, or subsequent events in the property's history.

**Home Management Applications** help owners organize their personal records. They are owner-centric — the records belong to the user account, not the property — and do not transfer at sale.

**Cloud Storage** provides personal record storage without organization, portability, or property-centric structure. Stored documents are records held by an individual — not an institutional property record.

**County Records** provide verified legal and fiscal records within a narrow scope. They are authoritative but incomplete — and disconnected from the physical history of the property.

**Insurance Records** contain rich records of a property's loss history — proprietary, carrier-fragmented, and inaccessible to the participants who need them most.

**Warranty Portals** track product-specific records associated with original purchasers — not connected to properties, not transferred at ownership change.

The consistent pattern: every existing solution holds records within the context of the institution or transaction that generated them. None is designed to assemble, connect, and preserve records across sources and across time into a persistent institutional record of the property.

## 11. Why the Gap Has Persisted

That a $45 trillion asset market can sustain this infrastructure gap requires explanation.

**No participant owns the problem.** The cost is distributed across buyers, sellers, lenders, insurers, appraisers, inspectors, contractors, and municipalities. No single party bears enough of the cost — or captures enough of the benefit — to justify building comprehensive infrastructure alone. This is a classic collective action failure.

**The market is organized around transactions, not properties.** Every major participant in residential real estate derives value from transactions. Their systems, incentives, and business models are oriented toward facilitating the next transaction — not assembling and preserving a persistent record of the property the transaction involves.

**Record systems are institution-centric, not property-centric.** Every system that touches a residential property organizes its records around its own operational needs. The property is a reference object. Building property-centric infrastructure requires a reorientation that no existing participant has had reason to undertake.

**No shared record standard exists.** Unlike financial services — where regulatory requirements have driven substantial data standardization — residential real estate operates without shared standards for physical property records. Without standards, a persistent institutional record cannot emerge consistently across markets or participants.

**The coordination cost is high.** Assembling a persistent institutional record requires connecting records across parties with different systems, different data standards, different competitive interests, and different regulatory environments. No single actor has had the scope to absorb that coordination cost.

**The cost of its absence is invisible.** Markets adapt to their constraints. Buyers price in uncertainty. Lenders build it into underwriting standards. Insurers build it into premium models. The cost is real but embedded — absorbed as a normal operating assumption rather than identified as a solvable structural problem. Invisible costs do not attract infrastructure investment.

## 12. The Infrastructure Gap: What a Persistent Institutional Record Requires

What the market is missing is not another marketplace, portal, or application. It is a registry — a specific category of infrastructure with specific characteristics that no existing solution provides.

Such infrastructure would need to:

**Treat the property as the primary entity.** Not the owner. Not the transaction. Not the organization. The property — with a persistent identifier that survives ownership changes, address changes, and legal boundary modifications.

**Assemble records from every participant.** Homeowners, contractors, inspectors, manufacturers, municipalities, and insurers each hold fragments of a property's history. The registry assembles from all of them — without privileging any single source and without requiring any single participant to provide everything.

**Preserve continuity across ownership.** A persistent institutional record that belongs to the property — rather than to any participant — is the only kind that can survive the changes of ownership, contractor relationships, insurance carriers, and organizational forms that a property will experience across its life.

**Accumulate across time.** The value of a property's assembled record grows with every record added and every year that passes. Infrastructure designed for a five-year horizon will not serve a fifty-year asset. Infrastructure designed to accumulate across decades serves every participant at every stage.

**Be independent of any single owner, transaction, or organization.** Institutional records held by any individual participant will be abandoned when that participant's relationship with the property ends. Only a record that belongs to the property itself — independent of every participant — can persist across the full lifecycle of the asset.

These are not novel characteristics. Persistent institutional records of this kind exist for vehicles, aircraft, financial instruments, medical devices, and legal title. The application of these principles to the physical and maintenance lifecycle of residential properties is the gap the market has never filled.

The underlying principle extends beyond residential real estate. Any long-lived physical asset of economic consequence eventually requires a persistent institutional record. Haus Registry is built for residential properties. The principle is universal.

## 13. Conclusion

The residential real estate market operates without a foundational layer of infrastructure: a persistent institutional record for the properties that constitute its primary assets.

This is not a product gap. It is not a feature gap. It is an infrastructure gap — the absence of a system responsible for assembling, preserving, and transferring the accumulated records of every residential property across ownership cycles and across generations.

Records about every home are continuously created. They are created by contractors, homeowners, inspectors, insurers, municipalities, and manufacturers — every day, across every property in the country. The market does not fail to generate records. It fails to carry them forward.

Every ownership transition severs the threads connecting those records to the property they document. The next owner inherits a home without its history. They pay to locate what already existed. They commission inspections that partially repeat prior inspections. They request permits that were already retrieved. They call contractors who repeat diagnostics already performed. The records that would have prevented all of this existed — fragmented, institution-specific, and inaccessible.

The consequences are systemic. Every major inefficiency in residential real estate follows predictably from the absence of a persistent institutional record. These are not separate problems requiring separate solutions. They are symptoms of one structural failure.

What is required is a registry — a persistent, property-centric record of a home's physical lifecycle, capable of assembling records from every participant, preserving them across every ownership cycle, and making them accessible to every participant who will ever have legitimate need of them.

Residential real estate has evolved with legal registries, tax registries, and ownership registries.

It has never developed a persistent institutional record of the property's physical lifecycle.

That is the missing infrastructure.

That is what Haus Registry exists to build.

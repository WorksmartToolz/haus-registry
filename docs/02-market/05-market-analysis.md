## A Note on Market Sizing for a Category That Does Not Yet Exist

Haus Registry is not entering an existing market. It is creating a new category of
infrastructure — the persistent institutional record layer for residential real estate.
No market research firm tracks this category because no company has built it yet.

This is not a weakness in the market analysis. It is the defining characteristic of
infrastructure companies. Carfax did not enter an existing "vehicle history report"
market. FICO did not enter an existing "credit score" market. Stripe did not enter
an existing "internet payments infrastructure" market. Each of these companies
constructed their TAM from the adjacent markets whose inefficiencies they eliminated.

We do the same here.

The Total Addressable Market for Haus Registry is constructed from the aggregate
value at stake across every stakeholder whose cost structure, pricing model, or
decision-making process is affected by the absence of a persistent institutional
property record. The Serviceable Addressable Market reflects the portion reachable
through our initial go-to-market approach. The Serviceable Obtainable Market
reflects what we can realistically capture in years 1 through 3.

**Data labeling convention used throughout this document:**
- **[Documented]** — Directly sourced from named research, government data, or
  industry association reports
- **[Constructed]** — Calculated from documented inputs with stated assumptions
- **[Reasoned]** — Directional estimate based on documented industry patterns
  with logic shown; no specific source available

## 1. The Asset Base: What We Are Building Infrastructure For

### US Residential Real Estate: The Primary Asset

**[Documented]** The total value of US residential properties reached $55.1 trillion
in 2025, according to Zillow analysis covering approximately 99 million residential
properties. This represents a $20 trillion increase since the eve of the pandemic
in early 2020.

**[Documented]** The US residential real estate market transaction volume is
estimated at $3.81 trillion in 2026, growing at a 2.04% CAGR through 2031,
according to Mordor Intelligence.

**[Documented]** Annual home sales volume: existing home sales reached approximately
4.17 million units in May 2026 at a seasonally adjusted annual rate (Trading
Economics, June 2026). Total annual transactions including new construction
run between 4.5 and 5.5 million per year in the current market.

**[Documented]** Median existing home sale price: approximately $407,500 in 2024
(Statista), rising modestly through 2025 and 2026.

**[Documented]** 65.3% of occupied US housing units were owner-occupied in 2024,
representing approximately 86 million owner-occupied homes (US Census Bureau).

**[Documented]** The average US homeowner holds a property for approximately 8 years.
With ~86 million owner-occupied homes, this implies approximately 10–11 million
ownership transitions per year at steady state — a figure suppressed in recent
years by the mortgage rate lock-in effect but expected to recover as rates normalize.

### The Infrastructure Opportunity

A $55.1 trillion asset class with 86 million individual units, each turning over
on average every 8 years, each generating continuous maintenance and improvement
activity, and each currently operating without a persistent institutional record —
is the asset base Haus Registry is building infrastructure for.

The opportunity is not the transaction. The opportunity is the property across its
entire lifecycle: the years of maintenance, improvement, repair, and documentation
that occur between transactions, and the moment of transaction when that
accumulated record becomes most valuable.

## 2. Total Addressable Market (TAM)

The TAM is constructed from five value pools that Haus Registry's infrastructure
directly affects. Each pool represents economic value currently lost, mispriced,
or inefficiently recovered due to the absence of a persistent institutional record.

### Value Pool 1: Transaction Due Diligence Friction

**[Documented]** The home inspection industry generates approximately $4.5 billion
annually in the US (Verified Market Reports, 2024). Over 3 million inspections are
conducted annually at an average cost of approximately $340–500 per inspection.

**[Documented]** Industry professionals estimate that 20–40% of inspection time
is spent assessing conditions and systems that prior accessible documentation
would already have addressed (Gitnux, industry survey data).

**[Constructed]** At 30% average time duplication across $4.5 billion in annual
inspection spend, approximately $1.35 billion in annual inspection effort
represents repeated discovery of previously known information.

**[Documented]** NAR documents that the average home sale involves 20–30 days
of due diligence. Documentation-related delays add an estimated 5–10 days to
the average transaction timeline.

**[Constructed]** At an average home price of $407,500, carrying costs of ~0.5%
per month, and 5 million annual transactions, each additional week of delay costs
approximately $500 per transaction: $2.5 billion in annual carrying cost
attributable to documentation reconstruction.

**Value Pool 1 Total (Constructed): ~$3.85 billion annually**

### Value Pool 2: Home Improvement Value Discounting

**[Documented]** US homeowner spending on improvements and repairs reached
approximately $531 billion in 2025 (Statista). Harvard's Joint Center for Housing
Studies projects total homeowner remodeling spending to reach $518 billion by
end of 2026.

**[Documented]** Real estate appraisers consistently apply lower value adjustments
to improvements that cannot be substantiated with accessible records. Discounts
of 10–30% relative to documented equivalents are consistent with practitioner
experience (Missing Memory Layer, §9 — industry practitioner estimate).

**[Constructed]** The professionally managed (Do-It-For-Me) segment of home
improvement — work performed by contractors that generates invoices, permits,
and documentable records — represents approximately $361 billion of the total
market (GMInsights, 2024). Assuming 25% of this work is undocumented or
inadequately documented, and that undocumented improvements are valued at a
20% discount vs. documented equivalents, the annual value gap attributable to
missing documentation is approximately $18 billion.

This is a conservative construction. It assumes only a fraction of work is
inadequately documented and a moderate discount rate. The true figure is likely
larger.

**Value Pool 2 Total (Constructed): ~$18 billion annually**

### Value Pool 3: Insurance Underwriting Uncertainty Premium

**[Documented]** The US homeowners insurance market reached $175.1 billion in
premium volume in 2025 (IBISWorld). Average annual premiums rose to approximately
$2,072 in 2024, up 20% from 2023 (Rate.com). Premiums have increased 78% over
the past six years.

**[Documented]** Insurance carriers are increasingly using AI-driven inspections,
satellite imagery, and drone assessments to evaluate homes and set prices based
on current property conditions — explicitly moving underwriting away from
assumptions and toward property-level data (Matic, 2025 Home Insurance Trends).

**[Documented]** The insurance industry's combined ratio for homeowners insurance
was 105.7% in 2024 — meaning insurers paid out more in claims and expenses than
they earned in premiums. A primary driver: inability to accurately price individual
risk without property-level history.

**[Reasoned]** If verified property maintenance records reduced claims-related
losses by a conservative 3–5% through more accurate underwriting, reduced disputes,
and incentivized maintenance, the value of that reduction against $175 billion
in annual premiums would represent $5.25–8.75 billion in annual economic value.
Haus Registry does not capture this value directly — but it is the value proposition
that makes Registry data worth paying for by insurance carriers and the reduction
in premiums that accrues to well-documented homeowners.

**Value Pool 3 Total (Reasoned): ~$5–9 billion annually**

### Value Pool 4: Mortgage Underwriting Uncertainty

**[Documented]** Total single-family mortgage origination volume is expected to
reach $2.2 trillion in 2026, up from $2.0 trillion in 2025 (Mortgage Bankers
Association, October 2025).

**[Documented]** Lenders commission appraisals, title searches, and condition
reports during underwriting — each a point-in-time assessment that cannot fully
account for the property's history. Residual uncertainty about physical condition
and maintenance history is priced into underwriting standards and interest rates.

**[Reasoned]** A conservative estimate: if verified property records reduced
lender risk assessment costs by 0.5–1.0 basis points on origination costs, or
allowed even modest improvements in pricing accuracy across a $2 trillion
origination market, the annual economic value would range from $1–2 billion.
The more significant opportunity is in secondary market pricing of mortgage-backed
securities, where property-level data quality directly affects risk pricing —
a market whose scale dwarfs origination volumes.

**Value Pool 4 Total (Reasoned): ~$1–2 billion annually in direct origination
impact; secondary market impact is a future platform opportunity**

### Value Pool 5: Warranty and Manufacturer Value Leakage

**[Documented]** Industry surveys consistently find that a majority of homeowners
are unaware of active warranties on systems and appliances in their homes.
Warranty records exist at the manufacturer level but are not connected to the
property in an accessible, persistent form.

**[Reasoned]** The US home warranty market and manufacturer warranty coverage
for installed home systems represents tens of billions in outstanding coverage
annually. The precise value of unclaimed warranty coverage is not tracked at
an industry level, but given the prevalence of warranty unawareness and the
cost of covered system replacements (HVAC systems: $5,000–15,000; roofs:
$10,000–30,000; water heaters: $1,000–3,500), the aggregate value of
unclaimed or lapsed coverage is material to homeowners.

**Value Pool 5 Total (Reasoned): Significant but not precisely quantifiable
from available data; treated as directional upside in the TAM**

|---|---|---|
| Transaction Due Diligence Friction | Constructed | ~$3.85B |
| Home Improvement Value Discounting | Constructed | ~$18B |
| Insurance Underwriting Uncertainty | Reasoned | ~$5–9B |
| Mortgage Underwriting Uncertainty | Reasoned | ~$1–2B |
| Warranty Value Leakage | Reasoned | Directional |

**TAM: $28–33 billion annually (Constructed)**

This figure represents the aggregate economic value at stake across the stakeholders
whose decisions and cost structures are most directly affected by the absence of
a persistent institutional property record. It is the ceiling of what infrastructure
in this category could eventually capture — not what Haus Registry will charge.

Infrastructure companies typically capture a small fraction of the economic value
they enable. Carfax captures a fraction of the value created by vehicle history
transparency. FICO captures a fraction of the value created by credit scoring.
The appropriate capture rate for Haus Registry will be defined in the Business
Model and Monetization Strategy documents. The TAM establishes that the underlying
value being created is large enough to support a significant business.

## 3. Serviceable Addressable Market (SAM)

The SAM reflects the portion of the TAM reachable through Haus Registry's initial
go-to-market approach: US residential properties, with primary entry through
homeowner adoption and transaction-adjacent touchpoints.

### SAM Inputs

**Transaction-adjacent entry (near-term primary channel):**

**[Documented]** Approximately 4.5–5.5 million residential transactions occur
annually in the current market. Each transaction creates a natural moment of
demand for property records — by buyers conducting due diligence, sellers
documenting improvements, lenders underwriting collateral, and agents preparing
listings.

**[Constructed]** If Haus Registry captures 20% of all residential transactions
as Registry Report touchpoints within 5 years — a penetration rate comparable
to Carfax in the vehicle market during its growth phase — that represents
approximately 900,000–1.1 million transaction-adjacent interactions annually.
At an average Registry Report value of $50–200 (for buyers, sellers, and
lenders), this represents $45–220 million in transaction-adjacent revenue potential.

**Homeowner direct (medium-term primary channel):**

**[Documented]** There are approximately 86 million owner-occupied US homes.
**[Documented]** Approximately 48% of homeowners planned renovations in 2025
(Fixr), creating natural entry points for Registry Vault and My Registry adoption.

**[Constructed]** If My Registry achieves 2% penetration of owner-occupied
homes within 5 years — 1.72 million active Property Registries — at an average
annual subscription of $50–120 per year, this represents $86–206 million in
annual subscription revenue.

**Professional participant channel (medium-to-long term):**

Registry Pro serving contractors, inspectors, appraisers, and insurers represents
a B2B revenue layer. With over 25,000 licensed home inspectors (documented),
726,000 remodeling businesses (documented, Fixr 2025), and approximately 2
million licensed real estate professionals in the US, the professional participant
addressable market is large.

**[Constructed]** If Registry Pro achieves 1% penetration of professional
participants within 5 years at an average of $500–2,000 per year, this represents
$14–57 million in annual professional revenue.

### SAM Summary

**[Constructed]** Serviceable Addressable Market (5-year horizon): **$145–483 million
in annual revenue** across transaction-adjacent, homeowner direct, and professional
participant channels.

This range reflects genuine uncertainty about pricing, penetration rate, and
channel mix — all of which will be refined through early market testing. The
range is intentionally wide; the Business Model document will narrow it with
specific pricing and penetration assumptions.

## 4. Serviceable Obtainable Market (SOM)

The SOM represents what Haus Registry can realistically capture in years 1–3,
assuming a focused launch in a defined initial market.

### Year 1–3 Assumptions

**Launch market:** One or two metropolitan markets with high transaction volume,
tech-forward homeowner demographics, and strong inspector/contractor ecosystems.
Candidates include Austin TX, Denver CO, Raleigh NC, and Nashville TN — markets
with active real estate turnover, high homeowner improvement spend, and above-
average digital adoption.

**Primary year 1–2 acquisition mechanism:** Transaction-adjacent — Registry Reports
ordered at the point of sale, similar to how Carfax reports became standard
in vehicle transactions.

**Primary year 2–3 acquisition mechanism:** Homeowner direct — My Registry
subscriptions driven by maintenance reminder value, warranty organization, and
contractor history.

**[Constructed]** Year 1 target: 5,000–10,000 active Property Registries in
launch market. At $50–120 annual average revenue per Registry, this represents
$250,000–$1.2 million in year 1 ARR.

**[Constructed]** Year 3 target: 50,000–150,000 active Property Registries across
2–3 markets, with growing transaction-adjacent and professional revenue. At
blended average revenue of $75–150 per Registry plus transaction and professional
revenue: $5–25 million in ARR by end of year 3.

**SOM: $5–25 million ARR by year 3 (Constructed)**

This is a wide range that will tighten as pricing and acquisition cost data
emerges from early market testing. The lower bound assumes slow consumer adoption
with minimal professional channel contribution. The upper bound assumes strong
transaction-adjacent adoption and early professional participant traction.

## 5. Market Structure

### Residential Real Estate: A Fragmented, Transaction-Oriented Industry

The residential real estate market is large but structurally fragmented. No single
participant controls the full transaction experience, and no participant has
sufficient scope to build and maintain a persistent institutional record for
properties. The structural characteristics that make the market large also make
the infrastructure gap persistent.

**Transaction volume:** 4.5–5.5 million annual transactions, each generating
significant documentation activity but no persistent record accumulation.

**Professional ecosystem:** Approximately 2 million licensed real estate agents,
25,000+ licensed inspectors, 726,000 remodeling businesses, and hundreds of
thousands of licensed contractors — all of whom generate records that currently
live in their own systems.

**Aging housing stock:** The median age of US homes exceeds 40 years (GMInsights,
2024). Older homes have longer, more complex maintenance histories — and therefore
more need for a persistent record layer. This structural characteristic grows
more favorable over time, not less.

**Geographic fragmentation:** Real estate is hyperlocal. County assessors, permit
offices, and MLS systems are organized at the local level. This fragmentation
is a barrier to entry for any solution that requires comprehensive local coverage —
and an advantage for a property-centric registry that aggregates records from
all sources without requiring local organizational infrastructure.

### The Property Data Market: Emerging but Incomplete

A secondary market in property data exists — Zillow, CoreLogic, ATTOM Data, and
others aggregate and sell property information. This market serves different
purposes than Haus Registry.

Existing property data companies are organized around transactions, valuations,
and market analytics. They aggregate historical sales data, public records, and
listing data to answer questions about market value and market conditions. They
do not assemble property-level maintenance histories, verified improvement records,
or the kind of lifecycle documentation that supports underwriting decisions.

Haus Registry is not competing with these companies for the same data or the same
customers. We are building the layer underneath — the persistent property record
from which other platforms, lenders, insurers, and appraisers can eventually draw.

## 6. Growth Vectors

Several structural forces are expanding the opportunity for Haus Registry
specifically — independent of overall real estate market growth.

### AI Document Processing Maturity

The capability to extract, classify, and structure property records from unstructured
documents — invoices, permits, inspection reports, warranty cards, photographs —
has reached practical maturity. This eliminates the primary technical barrier
that made comprehensive property record assembly impractical a decade ago.
Registry AI can now do in seconds what would have required hours of manual data
entry, fundamentally changing the economics of record assembly.

### Insurance Industry Crisis Creating Demand for Property Data

**[Documented]** US homeowners insurance premiums increased 78% over six years
through 2024 (Rate.com). The insurance industry is actively moving underwriting
away from actuarial models and toward property-level data — using AI-driven
inspections, satellite imagery, and drone assessments (Matic, 2025).

This crisis creates urgent demand, not just latent demand, for verified property
condition data. Insurers who can access Registry data will be able to price
risk more accurately and compete for well-maintained properties. This dynamic
makes insurance a near-term B2B revenue opportunity, not a long-term one.

### Aging Housing Stock and Deferred Maintenance Backlog

**[Documented]** The median age of US homes exceeds 40 years. The DIFM
(professionally managed) home improvement segment was valued at $361 billion
in 2024 and is projected to grow substantially through 2034 (GMInsights).

Older homes require more maintenance. More maintenance generates more records.
More records create more value in a persistent registry. The aging of the US
housing stock is a structural tailwind that compounds over the life of the company.

### Mortgage Market Normalization

**[Documented]** Total single-family mortgage originations are projected to
reach $2.2 trillion in 2026, up from $2.0 trillion in 2025, with purchase
originations driving growth as rates normalize toward the high 5% range.

Higher transaction volume means more natural entry points for Registry Reports
and more homeowners motivated to document improvements before listing. As the
mortgage rate lock-in effect eases and transaction volume recovers toward
historical norms, the transaction-adjacent acquisition channel grows.

### Digital Homeowner Adoption

The shift to digital home management accelerated during the pandemic and has
not reversed. Homeowners are comfortable with mobile-first applications for
managing home-related tasks, engaging contractors digitally, and storing
documents in the cloud. The friction of onboarding to My Registry is lower
today than it would have been five years ago, and it is declining further.

## 7. Structural Tailwinds Specific to Haus Registry

Beyond general growth vectors, several structural forces specifically favor
the infrastructure approach Haus Registry is taking.

### The Lock-In Effect of First-Mover Infrastructure

Infrastructure companies that establish the canonical record for an asset class
create compounding competitive advantages. Once a Property Registry exists for
a home, the switching cost of moving to a different registry is the loss of
accumulated history. Every year of documented history makes the existing Registry
more valuable and the prospect of starting over less attractive.

This is the same dynamic that makes Carfax dominant and credit bureaus nearly
impossible to displace. We are not just building a product with network effects.
We are building a record that compounds in value over time and becomes harder
to displace with every record added.

### Regulatory Momentum for Property Documentation

**[Documented]** Multiple US states are passing legislation requiring inspection
as a condition of sale, reversing a pandemic-era trend of inspection waivers
(Mordor Intelligence, Building Inspection Services, 2026). California's SB-721
requires deck inspections for multifamily properties. Oregon, Massachusetts, and
other states are enacting similar requirements.

Regulatory requirements for property documentation create forced adoption of
record-generating activities. Every mandated inspection is a record that should
live in a Property Registry.

### Insurance Market Structural Reform

The insurance industry's response to the profitability crisis is explicitly
moving toward property-level data. Carriers are investing in AI-driven
inspections, satellite monitoring, and behavioral data collection at the
property level. These investments create a market that is ready to pay for
verified property records — not just in the future, but now.

### The Generational Wealth Transfer

An estimated $84 trillion in wealth is expected to transfer between generations
in the US over the next 20 years, with residential real estate representing a
significant portion. Properties transferred between generations create natural
moments of documentation need — and properties with complete Registry records
will be demonstrably easier to transfer, insure, and sell than those without.

## 8. Market Risks

An honest market analysis identifies the forces that could constrain growth,
not only those that accelerate it.

**Transaction volume suppression:** The mortgage rate lock-in effect has
suppressed transaction volume below historical norms. If rates remain elevated
for longer than expected, the transaction-adjacent acquisition channel generates
fewer natural entry points. Mitigation: the homeowner direct channel and
professional channel are independent of transaction volume.

**Consumer willingness to pay for documentation:** Homeowners have not historically
paid for organizational tools at scale. The value proposition of My Registry
must be compelling enough to overcome the inertia of existing (inadequate)
documentation habits. Mitigation: the product must solve an immediate, felt
problem — not just a future one. Registry Alerts, warranty organization, and
contractor history are immediate value drivers.

**Data collection friction:** The value of a Property Registry grows with the
completeness of its records. A sparse Registry is less useful than a complete
one, creating a cold-start challenge for individual properties. Mitigation:
pre-population from public records (permits, deeds, assessor data), Registry AI
document extraction from homeowner uploads, and professional participant
contributions reduce cold-start friction significantly.

**Regulatory risk:** Property data touches privacy, real estate disclosure law,
and financial services regulation. Homeowner-submitted data, lender access to
property records, and insurance underwriting use of Registry data all require
careful navigation of applicable regulations. This is addressed fully in the
Regulatory Considerations document.

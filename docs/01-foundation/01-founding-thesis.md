## The Moment

Eight years after a repair, a replacement, or a service call — the question is always the same:

*"Where did I put that receipt?"*

Not because the homeowner was careless.  
Not because the contractor was unprofessional.  
Not because the work wasn't done.

But because no infrastructure existed to remember it.

The invoice went into a drawer, an email inbox, a filing cabinet, or a photo on a phone.  
The warranty card was mailed in — or wasn't.  
The permit was approved — and then disappeared into a county database no one knows how to access.  
The contractor who did the work moved, retired, or closed their business.  
The next owner of the home inherited none of it.

And so every homeowner, in every home, in every city, starts from zero.  
Every time.

This is not a personal failure.  
This is a structural one.

---

## The Thesis

**Residential real estate is the largest asset most people will ever own — and it is the only asset of its size that carries no persistent, verified record of its own history.**

A car has a VIN. That VIN follows the vehicle through every owner, every accident, every service visit, every title transfer. Carfax built a billion-dollar business on that single insight.

A person has a credit file. That file follows them through every loan, every payment, every financial decision they make. FICO and the credit bureaus became foundational infrastructure on that single insight.

A software project has a repository. Every change, every contributor, every decision is logged permanently. GitHub became the connective tissue of the entire technology industry on that single insight.

A home has nothing.

No persistent identifier that survives ownership changes.  
No verified record of what was built, repaired, replaced, or upgraded.  
No trusted source of truth for what happened — and when — and by whom.  
No infrastructure that makes the history of a home more valuable over time.

**Haus Registry exists to build that infrastructure.**

---

## The Structural Inefficiency

Today, the complete history of a residential property is fragmented across:

- County recorder offices
- Municipal permitting departments
- Contractor invoices and work orders
- Homeowner filing cabinets and email inboxes
- Warranty registration cards and manufacturer databases
- Inspection reports delivered as PDFs and never seen again
- Insurance claim records locked inside carrier systems
- Appliance manuals stuffed in kitchen drawers
- HOA records in spreadsheets
- Real estate transaction disclosures that become legally unusable after closing

Every time a home changes hands — which happens on average every 8 years in the United States — almost all of this information disappears.

The buyer starts over.  
The inspector rediscovers what was already known.  
The lender estimates what could have been documented.  
The insurer prices risk without the history that would reduce it.  
The appraiser guesses at improvements that were never recorded.  
The contractor repeats work that was already done — or undoes work that was done wrong.

This is not a technology problem.  
It is an infrastructure problem.

The technology to solve it has existed for years.  
What has never existed is the will to build the permanent layer underneath.

---

## The Belief

We believe:

**1. The home — not the owner — should be the primary entity.**

Ownership is temporary. The home is permanent. Every record, every event, every document should belong to the property — not to the person who owned it at the time.

**2. History is an asset.**

A home with a complete, verified record of its maintenance, repairs, and improvements is objectively worth more than an identical home with no history. Today, that value is invisible because no trusted record exists to make it visible.

**3. Verified information is infrastructure.**

The moment a buyer, lender, insurer, or appraiser can trust a property's record — without having to rediscover it — the entire transaction becomes faster, cheaper, and less uncertain. That trust, at scale, is infrastructure.

**4. The record should build itself.**

Homeowners should not be asked to maintain a database. The platform should capture information that already exists — permits, warranties, invoices, inspection reports, contractor records — during the normal course of homeownership. Documentation is a byproduct of living in a home, not an additional job.

**5. Trust compounds.**

Every verified record added to a Property Registry increases the value of every other record in it. A roof replacement documented by a licensed contractor, linked to a permit, photographed, and timestamped is not just a data point — it is evidence. Evidence compounds. Evidence is what makes infrastructure.

## Why Now

Three forces have converged to make this the right moment to build:

**AI document processing has matured.**  
Receipts, invoices, inspection reports, and warranty cards can now be extracted, classified, and structured automatically with high accuracy. The manual data entry problem that made this impossible a decade ago is now solvable.

**Homeowners are more digital than ever.**  
The COVID era accelerated home improvement spending and digital adoption simultaneously. Homeowners are comfortable managing home-related tasks on mobile devices, and the contractor ecosystem has moved substantially online.

**Trust in real estate data is at a low point.**  
Buyers, sellers, and professionals across the industry have grown skeptical of estimated values, unverified disclosures, and opaque transaction data. The market is ready for a source of truth it can actually rely on.

## What We Are Not Building

This clarity is as important as the thesis itself.

We are not building a listing platform.  
Listings are temporary. We are building something permanent.

We are not building a home valuation tool.  
Estimates are opinions. We are building verified history.

We are not building a contractor marketplace.  
Marketplaces optimize for transactions. We are building records.

We are not building another home management app.  
Apps solve convenience problems. We are solving an infrastructure problem.

We are not competing with Zillow, Redfin, or the MLS.  
Those platforms are built on listings. We are built on the property itself.

---

## The Company We Are Building

Haus Registry is being built as infrastructure.

Not as a consumer app that might become infrastructure someday.  
As infrastructure from the first line of code.

Every design decision, every data model, every trust framework, every API contract should be made as if this system will underpin residential real estate transactions for the next fifty years.

Because if we build it right — it will.

The Property Registry is not a product feature.  
It is a new category of asset record.

The Registry ID is not a database key.  
It is a permanent identifier for a piece of the built world.

The Registry Confidence Score is not a UI element.  
It is a trust signal that lenders, insurers, and appraisers will eventually depend on.

The Registry Report is not a PDF export.  
It is the most complete, verified account of a property's history ever assembled.

---

## The Founding Commitment

We will build with the discipline that infrastructure demands.

We will never sacrifice verified truth for more data.  
We will never optimize for growth at the expense of trust.  
We will never treat the homeowner's information as a product to be sold without their knowledge.  
We will never pretend to know something we don't.

Every record will show its source.  
Every claim will show its confidence.  
Every edit will remain auditable.  
Nothing will be overwritten.  
Everything will be versioned.

Because the value of a registry is not in what it contains today.  
It is in the fact that it has always been there —  
and always will be.
